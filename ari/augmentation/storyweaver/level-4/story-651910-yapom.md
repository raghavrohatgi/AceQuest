# Yapom

The grass used to be green; the trees were spread all over. There was a forest here, once. There was much din and chatter.&nbsp; I move from here to there. I am a homeless wanderer. I stare all around me and still wonder when all this changed.

Round and round his bicycle goes, I see his ane and abo leave each morning in their big, big cars.&nbsp; Does he not miss his parents? Does he not feel my presence?

Oh, he is looking around. I wonder… Does he see me? He sure reminds me of someone. My son. Oh, how my heart aches for my little one. I wonder if he is playing somewhere too.

I should not have left him alone. I should not have let him go.

Is it me he is talking to? He can see me? Oh, how wonderful is that! “Who… who are you?”

“Come here,” I gently swoop up this child.

“Where am I? Where am I?” he frantically cries. Why is he crying? What have I done? I only wanted to have some fun.

But the boy keeps shouting, “Let me go!”&nbsp; Ah! He is afraid. “You are free to go,”&nbsp; I calmly say. I only wanted to talk,&nbsp; not harm him.

“Who are you?” he asks again.

“I am a yapom . I’ve known you long. Your house is where we once lived by.” “Really? How?&nbsp; That’s my HOUSE?” he gushes.

“I was here long before any of your ancestors,” I reply. “Friends and family moved away, but I could not leave it all behind.”

“Don’t you miss them?” leaves his lips, as he leans closer to me and&nbsp; offers his little hand.&nbsp; For so long, high up and lonely,&nbsp; I had been on the hirek tree.&nbsp; Has he guessed my need for company? “I do miss them, but I can’t leave for the unknown,” is my quiet response.

“My son, oh, he left with them, too. Thinking of him, I kept looking at you. That’s why, perched upon my tree’s branch, I have watched your daily dance.”

“Oh, I never knew that &nbsp;you were watching me, as I spent my days playing around.” “You should have come to me. We could have played many games. I wouldn’t have been so lonely then.”

“You say that now,&nbsp; but wouldn’t&nbsp; have so,&nbsp; when people around would have riled and raged.”

“Do you hear the car’s honk?”&nbsp; “Oh, I do. Sadly, I do.” He seems to want to linger,&nbsp; but he has to go. “You should go home, now.&nbsp; I’ll watch from afar.” *HONK!*

This day will be a blur to him. But I will keep this memory. And watch from afar, again. Waiting at my lonely tree.

Glossary • Ane: A galo word for Mother • Abo: A galo word for Father • Yapom: A supernatural spirit in the Galo Folklore from Arunachal Pradesh • Hirek: Banyan tree

About the Story This story takes the much-maligned figure of the yapom in folklore of the Galo tribe from Arunachal Pradesh and tries to reimagine the story from the perspective of this figure known for their supposed kidnappings of people. The yapoms have been depicted as nature/forest spirits in many narratives of the tribe. While their depiction has predominantly been that of malevolent spirits, there have been instances of exploring them as benevolent spirits, too. Their origin has been the topic of many studies, and it has been stated by many that they share the same ancestors as the people of the tribe.

Acknowledgement This story, while taking some creative liberties, has been finalised after extensive secondary research of the available literature on the yapoms . Besides that, one person who had been the victim of one of such kidnappings by a yapom in their childhood, was interviewed during the course of the research. Their identity has been kept confidential for reasons pertaining to privacy.
