# Kilbe

“Go away! Go away! Don’t come near us! ” “You go away! Don’t come here.”

Kengkong kengkong women beating the rice, Sripsrek sripsrip men making bamboo baskets, Ushers the dawn of the Cotton Bazaar season upon the village of Tekarachu Takilkila gre

The sun rays on her face riprap riprap Wakes our dear Otse from her sleep She walks towards the balcony as she trips tap… tip To a sight of a beautiful foliage. And the mountain and Kil Oljomas travelling to the bazaar&nbsp; like grai grai grai grai .&nbsp; A semal tree steals her gaze. As the wind blows srisri sra sra ...

Even after the passing of many ‘ dilip dilip’ and ‘gogarek gok ’ Otse kept picking up the &nbsp;bolchu in its season through all the moments of her life.

Otse was now wrinkled and old. Due, her grandchild, with a twinkle in her eyes squeezes&nbsp; the bolchu Otse gathered po’om po’om She asks, “Why do you collect bolchu , Ambi?” “Oh beloved Due, it taught me about love better than any one,&nbsp; didori didorido, ” replies Ambi Otse

One day, Otse didn't make it past the day. With bolchu in her hand she breathed her… Oang Oang

The ‘Imbeng’ was without a language but loved so faultless As Due bonded with it, she named it Kilbe, They bonded like the beautifully woven reking, taktak taktak

Due and Kil went on an errand for uncle Pintok to collect herbs from the forest…

Kil and Due on their search for herbs Saw a cave from afar, As they walk further and further towards it, it drizzles,&nbsp; then it rains heavier and heavier; and as they reach the mouth of the cave, a storm hits, going grimgrim graw graw.

“Go away! Don't come here,”&nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; a voice rages against the storm, But Kil with the water in its body sprouts flowers all over itself and points a bouquet at the voice squealing, “ imbeng imbeng. ”

A voice fell to light, revealing Tongte Gongge whose feet gongge .&nbsp; Sure, its tears turning to flowers made it feel understood, Prompting her to ask, “Do you want to play with me?” And Kil, “imbeng imbeng”

Glossary Tekarachu Takilkila Gre : A fictional village, inspired by the name of the mother of Tongte Gongge. Kengkong Kengkong: Sound of someone beating rice husks with a traditional A'chik rice pestle Sripsrek Sripsrip: Sound of someone making a bamboo basket. Kil Oljoma: Cotton merchant. Riprap Riprap: The sound made when a person rubs their eyes with their hands as a result of the strong rays of sunlight. Grai Grai Grai Grai: Sound of a crowd moving. Srisri Sra Sra: Sound of the wind. Toktak Toktak: Sound of water falling, signifying teardrops in the story. Bolchu: Semal cotton. Dikdik dikdik: Sound of a heart beating. Tap Tip: Sound of someone tripping. Drengchak jenggitchak Drengchak rakichak: Sound of drum playing.

Glossary Gogarekgok: Sound of a rooster crowing. Dilip Dilip: Sound of stars twinkling. Po'om Po'om: Sound of squeezing something soft, squeezy, and fluffy. Ambi:&nbsp; a Garo word for grandmother. Didori Didorido: Sound of the adil being played. Oang Oang: Sound of a big owl-like bird, which is said to&nbsp; predict the death of someone every time it ‘oangs’ . Apai Apai Amai Amai: Sound of grief. Imbeng Imbeng: Sound of Kilbe. It is a sound inspired by the&nbsp; sound of a musical instrument called gongbina or imbanggi. Taktak Taktak: &nbsp; Sound of someone working a loom. Dai Dai Dai Dai: Sound of a waterfall hitting or waves crashing. Mai Jing Jing Jing Jing: Sound of cicadas. Wapepe Pepe: Sound of whistles that a bamboo makes. Grimgrim grawgraw: Sound of storm. Tongte Gongge: A creature with a crooked or inverted feet. Gongge: &nbsp;Inverted.

About 'Kilbe' In the Garo Hills of Meghalaya, people believe in the existence of a mythical creature with gnome-like attributes and inverted feet. Locals commonly call this creature ‘Teteng’ and ‘Tongte tengte’ is a term used by many in written literature. This creature has numerous lores surrounding it. In the story ‘Kilbe,’ Tongte Gongge symbolises human nature and what it means to be human, both the good parts and the not-so-nice aspects. Two things inspired the author to write Kilbe –his sister collecting bolchu, and his own fear of something he felt he needed.&nbsp; This story is an original work of the author, and the onomatopoeic sounds used are subjective in nature. &nbsp; He hopes for the reader to enjoy the little pockets of treasures and untold stories that are hidden in the pages and phrases of this story. Like an octopus, the author has eaten his own hands working on this story, and he is very grateful to every reader who has chosen to read Kilbe .
