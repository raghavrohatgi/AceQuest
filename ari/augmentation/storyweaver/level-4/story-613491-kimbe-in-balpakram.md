# Kimbe in Balpakram

Kimbe looked around, floundering about in the air,&nbsp; quite confused at what was happening around her. She was floating, but she was also lying on her bed, unmoving. There were so many people around her as well. “Ah I feel so light. Where am I?”

"She was born with the first rays of the sun. She knew all her mother’s songs and the flowers in the fields… ” “She ran with the wind and loved the company of animals. Today her cow leads her to her final resting place in the Dal·gipa Nalsa, the Great Beyond." Ambi’s voice echoed through the entire house as she sang the ajea. “At least, it doesn’t hurt anymore,” Kimbe mumbled, as she floated towards the sound.

Everyone was there. " This is just like that time with Atchu, " Kimbe thought.&nbsp; And Atchu never came back. &nbsp; Looking at the tear stricken faces of her parents and siblings,&nbsp; K imbe sighed in understanding.&nbsp; “Ahh, this may be last time I see them.”&nbsp;

Kimbe watched Baba lead a calf to the sacrificial altar.&nbsp; She had spent a lot of time playing with the calf before she couldn't anymore.&nbsp; He was her dear companion.&nbsp; “Lead my daughter to the Dal·gipa Nalsa . Thank you for being her companion in her final journey,” she heard Baba whisper.

"I hope they won’t be too sad,”&nbsp; Kimbe thought as she found herself carried away into another world.

She looked around and wondered,&nbsp; "Is this Balpakram?" This land was just as magical as Ambi had described in her stories.&nbsp; The calf mooed by her side, almost as though he was nodding. Kimbe giggled at that. “Thank you for being with me,” she said, giving the calf a big hug.

Holding on to her companion by its rope, she was unsure where to go. Noticing her, the birds of paradise called out to her,&nbsp; “Come with us!”

They led her to the Chidimmak pool. “Come wash your ashes in my waters,” said the Chidimmak ,&nbsp; calling her towards itself. The calf mooed in agreement. &nbsp; Kimbe gently washed away the ashes from her body.

After she was done, the birds asked her to follow them again. “Where are you taking me?” she asked, curiously following them. The birds flew and perched on a tall, tall tree. Kimbe exclaimed, “This must be the Matchu Boldak Karam from Ambi’s stories!&nbsp; Wow! It’s beautiful!” “You’re right, little one ," said the tree in a voice as old as time and memory. " Come and tie your companion to my trunk, like all those before you have done.”&nbsp;

“Thank you for being my friend, Matchu ,”&nbsp; she whispered, as she leaned her head against his.&nbsp; He was her dear companion,&nbsp; even in her afterlife. &nbsp;“Moo,” the calf smiled. " Go along, little one ," the tree gently encouraged.

With closed eyes, she walked forward.&nbsp; &nbsp;And then she entered into the embrace of the Dal·gipa Nalsa, that welcomed her to a slumber beyond time.

Back in the village, embers whispered the tale of the fire and smoke that carried Kimbe’s spirit home, free and ethereal. Although she was gone, she would never be forgotten.

&nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; Glossary Ajea: &nbsp;Funeral dirge of the people of the Garo community. Ambi: A Garo word for grandmother. Atchu: A Garo word for grandfather. Matchu: A Garo word for a cow. Dal·gipa Nalsa:&nbsp; The Great Beyond/Afterlife according to the belief of the people in the Garo community.&nbsp; Chidimmak: &nbsp;The pool where spirits wash away the ashes of their cremation&nbsp; Matchu Boldak Karam:&nbsp; In the Balpakram park, there is a hard Schima wallichii tree that has a mysteriously acquired depression on its trunk, as if weathered and worn by ropes that have been tied around it. It is believed that spirits tether to the tree the animals that have been sacrificed during the funeral rites.

About Balpakram Balpakram, also called the "land of perpetual winds," is a national park located in the southern Garo Hills of Meghalaya, India. It is revered by the Garo tribe as the resting place of departed souls. Balpakram is shrouded in myths and&nbsp; &nbsp;leg ends. Garo folklore is deeply intertwined with Balpakram, with stories passed down through generations and narrated around the fire. Kimbe's adventure brings to life a vivid picture of the Garo spirit world, brimming with curiosity, wonder, and a touch of the unknown. It is a story to help understand the often-difficult truths about life and death. &nbsp;

Did you know? 1. Balpakram was declared as a National Park on December 27, 1987.&nbsp; It is &nbsp;located in the southern Garo Hills of Meghalaya, India . 2. Me · mang anti cha · ram in Balpakram is a market for spirits. The place features big fossilized rocks and footprints of animals and people. Fossilized seeds of plants can also be seen. 3. Dikki ni ring (Dikki’s boat) is a big stone in Balpakram that looks like a boat. Long ago, it flipped over in a storm and even now, it exists in an upside down position! 4. Balkol is a canyon in Balpakram where everything that gets thrown down, comes back up. If you throw a handkerchief, it’ll fly back up to you!
