# The tale of Pukshi

In the kingdom of Samutak, ruled by the great sorcerer Haokha&nbsp;Nongkha Ngamba, magic was as common as the air one breathed. He was powerful enough to shape the world around him, turning&nbsp;barren fields into fertile lands and stones into jewels.&nbsp; But his greatest treasure was his only daughter, Wandengnu. Wandengnu was a curious and adventurous soul.&nbsp; She loved to be in the fields, working alongside her friends, her heart as&nbsp; wild as the winds that swept through the hills of Samutak.

One day, as the rain clouds gathered, Wandengnu approached her father.&nbsp; “Father, I wish to go with my friends for Pam Yanba ,” she said. Haokha Nongkha was delighted, and he used his powers to turn&nbsp; a large pot of honey into a small gourd.

“Take this, my daughter,” he said. “Share it with your friends during&nbsp; lunch. It will give you strength for the task ahead.”&nbsp; “Thank you, Father.”

She ran to her friends who were waiting for her.

However, as she made her way to the hilltops with her friends, six boys and seven girls, a&nbsp; thought began to fester in her heart.&nbsp; She checked her basket and realised that the gourd was too small. It rolled around the&nbsp; bottom of her basket!

Such a small thing! And she had thirteen people to share it with. How could she divide it among so many? What if her friends tease her for being a miser?&nbsp; It wouldn’t be enough for anyone but her. “It’s just a few drops,” she thought.&nbsp; “Let me just drink it all.”

But as she drank, something strange happened.&nbsp; Grrrr, grrr, her stomach growled.&nbsp; Something started building up in her stomach. It was so uncomfortable. Unaware of her discomfort, her friends continued their chatter.

Soon, the discomfort became unbearable.&nbsp; She swelled, and swelled, and swelled until BOOM!

She exploded, showering the land in a&nbsp; storm of honey and gore.

The shock of her sudden death echoed through the&nbsp; hills. When Haokha Nongkha Ngamba arrived at the&nbsp; scene, he found only devastation. His daughter’s body had disintegrated into thousands of tiny, buzzing insects. &nbsp; &nbsp;

The insects swarmed over the fields, feasting&nbsp; on the paddy crops, destroying everything in&nbsp; their path. Their numbers grew exponentially,&nbsp; and the land, once fertile and abundant,&nbsp; became barren and desolate.

In his grief, Haokha Nongkha Ngamba used&nbsp; his final magic to trap the insects beneath a&nbsp; massive boulder, binding Wangdengnu to the&nbsp; very land that had given birth to them both.&nbsp; He succeeded a little, but the insects kept&nbsp; multiplying.

As any loving father would, Haokha Ngamba performed a ritual to&nbsp; calm his daughter’s restless spirit.&nbsp; “Oh, my beloved child, I am sorry that you have been taken from this&nbsp; world so soon. I am sorry I didn't&nbsp; warn you of my magic. Please&nbsp; forgive me, your sorrowful father.”

Wangdengnu’s spirit heard her father’s plea and appeared before him to speak&nbsp; her final words.&nbsp; “Father, it is my fault that I met such a tragic end. I wish I had not become this&nbsp; force of destruction.&nbsp; “With my powers, I will swallow all the pests back into my belly once more.”

The people of Samutak witnessed this and rejoiced. They came to worship her as a&nbsp; goddess, the Pukshi Goddess.&nbsp; During the sowing season, they offered their prayers, seeking her protection from the&nbsp; insects that bore her name, Pukshi - the one who died by the stomach.

Since then, in the lands around Wangoo, Sugnu, and Kakching, communities would&nbsp; gather each year to honour the Pukshi Goddess, offering their harvest and prayers in&nbsp; hopes that the Pukshi would spare their crops.

Glossary Pam Yanba: to clear the forest for jhum cultivation. Thirteen Friends: In ancient Meitei belief, travelling in groups of seven is considered a bad omen. In this story, the storyteller uses the phrase “hao nura taret pakhang taret” , meaning a group of six boys and seven girls, to foreshadow the tragic event that is about to unfold, warning the listeners of the impending misfortune. Samutak: Believed to be the ancient name of the place presently called Sumtuk. Pukshi: Pukshi refers to the collective name for common paddy pests found in Manipur. The shrine dedicated to Pukshi still stands at Kakching Khunou, situated atop a large boulder. This boulder is believed to have been used by Haokha Nongkha Ngamba to contain the pests.

About the story The legend of Pukshi has been passed down through the communities of Manipur for generations. The Pukshi Tin, or “Pukshi Pests” , refers to a group of insects that infest the paddy fields of Manipur. These seasonal pests can cause significant damage to crops, posing a challenge to the agricultural community. However, the indigenous people of Manipur find comfort in a tradition of worship, seeking to appease these natural forces by paying homage to sacred places within the environment. This practice is driven by the hope that through respect and reverence, their worries and suffering will be alleviated. The story of Pukshi reflects the traditional storytelling style of Manipur, weaving a cautionary tale that emphasises the importance of harmony with nature. It underscores the values of nature worship, reverence for the forces of the natural world, and the belief in the need for balance rather than retaliation or domination. Through this narrative, the message that humanity must live in harmony with the natural world, respecting its power and understanding its rhythms, is conveyed.
