# Story Chikkappa

Dedicated to the Gram Panchayat Librarians of Karnataka

Uma rings the big bell at the entrance. All the children rush inside for their favourite time of the day — the story hour. Vidya Akka draws a circle on the&nbsp; floor with a piece of chalk and all the&nbsp;children sit around her.

Today is Uma’s turn to pick her favourite book from the ones strung across the room on a rope. Uma S. .T. .R . .E . .T. .C. .H . .E . .S her hand, goes on&nbsp; her tippy toes, picks the one which has a picture of&nbsp; many books on its cover, and hands it over to&nbsp; Vidya Akka.

“Will you read us this story, Akka?” “Of course Uma, but first you must tell everyone what made you choose this book.” Uma points to the mysterious title and says, “THE LAND WITHOUT STORIES ! ! ! I can’t imagine what it would be like to have no stories in my life. I love stories!”

Vidya Akka asks the other children, “Can you imagine what it would feel like to live in a land without stories?” All the other children look at each other and, almost in chorus, repeat: “THE LAND WITHOUT STOOORIES?!!!” They look surprised!

Vidya Akka tries to get their attention. “Now, now, children, who would like to know more about&nbsp; this?” She says, “1, 2, 3... (ONDU, ERADU, MOORU),” and they all respond by saying, “SHURU MAADI!” “shuru maadi!” “shuru maadi!”

Vidya Akka’s voice suddenly dips&nbsp; and the chatter in the room&nbsp; settles down to make way for the&nbsp; story. SSSHHHHHHHH! “Imagine a place far far away. Everyone would wake up in the morning and spend their day working silently."

“The farmer silently tilled his land… TAK TAK TAK." “The postman silently opened the mailbox at home and slipped in the letters without saying a word. The only sound that was heard was when the mailbox closed... FLAP FLAP FLAP." TAK TAK TAK FLAP FLAP FLAP

“Even the animals and birds in this village were mostly silent. The children wandered around from street to street, looking bored and confused."

“When they went back home in the evening, they just ate their dinner quietly and went to sleep."

“Their parents were too tired after the day’s work and had no energy to remember any stories. And ajjas and ajjis too would just sit quietly in a corner of the house. It was as if someone had cast a spell on the&nbsp;people of this land.”

Guntu interrupted Vidya Akka, “Akka, Akka, I know! Like when we press the MUTE button on the TV remote control.” “Exactly Guntu, that is an interesting connection you made!” said Vidya Akka.

“Srinivas, the village librarian, also lived in this land. And, like the others, he too would wake up every morning and go to his library which was next to the panchayat office. He would SILENTLY dust the big shelves of storybooks and arrange the new books that arrived neatly on them."

“During the afternoons, he sat at his desk and looked outside the large window hoping that someone would come inside to read those books. And he would do that till evening. Then, at 5:00 p.m., he would put the big lock on the library office door&nbsp; and go home."

“One night, just before falling asleep, he thought to himself, ‘IT WOULD BE SO NICE IF CHILDREN CAME TO MY LIBRARY and listened to all those stories lying still inside those hundreds of books.’

“That night he had a dream. In his dream, he&nbsp; had become an eight-year-old child. Around&nbsp; him, there were many other children. They were looking at someone. But who was this?"

“She looked old. She was wearing a colourful robe and a strange looking conical hat with a bell hanging from its&nbsp; top. DING DING DONG! Her hands moved up and down in the air, and the look in her eyes changed every few seconds. All the children listened to her. Some giggled, others looked shocked. No one seemed bored or restless." ding ding dong

“The next morning, Srinivas woke up and looked puzzled. It seemed like he was trying to recall his dream. He got ready for work and spent his morning dusting&nbsp; books and writing something in his big fat notebook. Then, after lunch, he sat down at his desk and looked at&nbsp; the empty window. "

“He saw children wandering past the window. One of them came close to the window and peeped inside." 'COME! COME!'

“His mother stood a few steps behind him. Srinivas immediately called this boy inside, ‘COME! COME!’" “The boy was a little scared to go inside the library room but he was also curious. He looked back at his mother and called her close. They both entered the library."

“Srinivas asked him, ‘What is your name, little boy?’ ‘Appu,’ said the little one. Srinivas remembered the parcel&nbsp; of new books that had arrived&nbsp; in the library that morning. He asked Appu, ‘Would you like&nbsp; to listen to a story?’ Appu did not know what that meant but looked excited&nbsp; at the thought of something new to do.&nbsp; He nodded his head. ‘Come sit here, Appu,’ Srinivas said and began reading. ‘Once upon a time…’

“For the next thirty minutes, Appu could not take his eyes off Srinivas. Appu watched his hands move and sway as he narrated the story." “His face had so many expressions. His voice changed in so many different ways. I t was all so interesting. Appu had never heard or seen something like this before!" “For some time, he actually felt like he was&nbsp; a part of the story. And the story was about a little boy like him. At the end of the story when Appu was leaving, Srinivas told him, ‘Come again&nbsp; tomorrow.’"

“The next day in the afternoon, Appu&nbsp; was back at the library window. This time, along with him, four other faces eagerly peeped inside." “Srinivas called them in. Appu led his friends confidently. They enjoyed the story reading and promised to return the following day."

“On the third day, there was a crowd of children at the library entrance. Appu pointed to Srinivas and said, ‘Look! That is Story Chikkappa.’ Srinivas was delighted. The silent library room was filled with the laughter of children."

“Soon word spread in the Land Without Stories. All the villagers heard about Story Chikkappa and were curious to meet him."

“Mothers brought their little babies along to listen to stories. The local school invited Story Chikkappa to the school to narrate stories to children and, for those children who could not come to school, Story Chikkappa would go to their homes with a bundle of storybooks."

“Life in the Land Without Stories looked different The farmer’s wife told the farmer the story she had heard from Story Chikkappa." “The crow heard the story being told and went and cawed to the other crows. JUST LIKE AIR, THESE STORIES WERE TRAVELLING EVERYWHERE."

“Children were no longer wandering around in the afternoons. Older children started making up short stories on their own, after listening to so many from Chikkappa, and narrating them to younger children. By listening to these stories, children learnt how to laugh at themselves, fight&nbsp; imaginary monsters, and the secrets of life." Just like air, these stories were travelling everywhere!

“The Land Without Stories transformed into a LAND FULL OF STORY TREASURES, told by the children to their children, and later to their grandchildren.” Storytime at Shaanta Graama Library ends. Vidya Akka closes the book and asks Uma to put it back on the shelf. She asks the children, “Tell me what did you find most interesting in this story?”

Uma says, “I like that STORIES NEVER GROW OLD and, like air, stories can travel anywhere and everywhere. When I grow up, I also want to be like Story Chikkappa and spend my day telling stories to little children.”

The state of Karnataka has over 5600 gram panchayat libraries in rural areas. During the COVID-19 pandemic,&nbsp;Smt Uma Mahadevan-Dasgupta, who is the Additional Chief Secretary of the Panchayat Raj, saw the potential of&nbsp;these libraries to provide the children of rural communities with quality literature and reduce the learning loss&nbsp;that the pandemic had brought in its wake. She initiated the “Oduva Belaku” (The Light of Reading) program across Karnataka to revive rural public libraries. The objectives were to help children stay connected to reading and build a library culture within communities. Membership to the library was made free and children were issued library cards. Many libraries received a&nbsp;makeover and some were even given new spaces for better access. Digitization of the libraries was also taken up and many libraries were provided with computers, smart TVs and mobile phones as well. Ms Mahadevan-Dasgupta, who loved to read, was aware that only changes to the library facilities would not attract children. The librarians who ran these libraries also needed to learn new ways to make them child-friendly spaces. In order to do this, civil society organizations like Adhyayan Quality Education Foundation and others&nbsp;were invited to provide to help the librarians learn strategies to increase membership of these libraries and get more children to come to them. Adhyayan Foundation trained the librarians to conduct read-aloud sessions in their libraries for the children. This programme was a huge success — it started in two districts and, was quickly adopted by four more. It is now all&nbsp;set to expand to all the districts as it has helped both children and librarians discover the magical world of stories. About the Oduva Belaku Program

Glossary Gram Panchayat: “gram” means village and “panchayat” is a kind of a council or group of people who govern the village. Pandemic: the spread of a disease at the same time in many countries of the world. Oduva Belaku (The Light of Reading): the name of the programme launched by the Rural Development and Panchayat Raj Department in Karnataka to help the libraries in rural areas become joyful spaces for children. Civil Society Organizations: organizations that work without proìt to improve society. Read-aloud: A type of storytelling in which the reader expressively reads the story aloud to children, so that the story sounds interesting.
