# Yapa Yapa Yambuks

“Takar, come help me.”

“Something’s up,”&nbsp; said Oiyao, jumping up.&nbsp; “Look how busy they are!” Bangkon chuckled.&nbsp; “It's Ali-Aye Ligang , little one!”

“Huh? Are you sure?” Menam asked. “I am very sure.” Bangkon pointed at the decorations, his joints creaking. “These only come out during this time.” “What's Ali-Aye Ligang ?” Oiyao asked, wide-eyed. Appun chuckled, “It’s a festival, little one. This is when everyone prepares for the planting season, celebrating new beginnings and praying for a good harvest."

“A festival?” said Oiyao, “What happens at a festival? Is there a cake?” “Very sure,” Bangkon replied. “They are all talking about the food?” “No, no,” Menam said hurriedly, “No cake, but I have heard that there is lots of food, music, and dancing. If it is Ali-Aye Ligang , then it's a big celebration! It’s the most important time of the year. Are you really sure, Bangkon?”

Oiyao’s eyes sparkled. “Food! What kind of food?” Bangkon chuckled. “Oh, the best kind! There are plates and plates of&nbsp; purang, apin, and egg adin. ” “And don’t forget Lai hak, matimah, bogor i,” Appun added.&nbsp; “I heard them talking about making it this year.” “And so much apong,” Bangkon added. “Wow! That's such a party!” Oiyao’s eyes widened with excitement. “Yes, it is a big deal,” Appun says thoughtfully,&nbsp; “but this is nothing. In our time it used to be even bigger.&nbsp; Do you remember, Bangkon?” “Ah yes, so much dancing and fields full of rice.&nbsp; Those were the days.” “I wish I could have seen it instead of being locked away last year,” Menam sighed. “Locked away?!” Oiyao gasped. “Why?”

“You see,” Bangkon explained, “for the longest time, people from our community have lived near the river. They have learnt how it flows, and so they build their houses on stilts to stay safe from floods. However, last year…” “What?” Oiyao asked, “What happened last year?”

Bangkon sighed, glancing at the faint water stains on his wooden frame. “You see, over time, instead of understanding her flow, people have tried to control the river, and that’s made her angry,” Appun quietly explained. Bangkon sighed. “Last year, when the heavy rains came, the weak embankments broke, and the river flooded the village. Everything was washed away.” Menam added, “So they had to find&nbsp; a&nbsp; new place to stay.” “How terrible,” Oiyan whispered.

Bangkon smiled gently. “But the people are strong. They didn’t lose hope.&nbsp; They built new homes and held onto their culture and traditions.”

Appun grinned. “And now, they’re ready to celebrate Ali-Aye Ligang in their new homes! Even if it’s smaller, people still celebrate. They still plant seeds, cook delicious food, wear their best clothes and dance with joy.” Menam excitedly said,&nbsp; “Oiyao, grab that one!” “And now, I’m ready to dance!” “Haha! This is the best!”&nbsp; Oiyao was overjoyed.

“All this sounds good, but look at us,” Menam said, “With all the moving and settling in, they have forgotten about us. We are still stuck here as they prepare. I don’t think we will get to see anything this year.” The toys became silent because there was truth to those words.

“Don’t be sad, Menam. You know what?” said Oiyao, breaking the silence. “We should get ready, too! If they’re celebrating, we should be part of it!” “What do you mean?” Menam asks.

“Well, we should dress up!” Oiyao said, pointing at the fabric all around them. “Once they see how nice we look, they will have to take us along!”

Bangkon chuckled. “You’re right! We can’t just sit here looking plain while the whole house gets festive!”

“That sounds like a great plan!” Appun added.

“Alright then, I want a scarf!” Menam grabbed a piece of red cloth&nbsp; with&nbsp; black,&nbsp; white, and yellow stripes. “Perfect!” Appun said. Menam excitedly said, “Oiyao, grab that one!” “And now, I’m ready to dance!”

“You try this Bangkon.” Appun hands him an erpob. “How do I look?” Bangkon asked, striking a dramatic pose. “Like a festival elder!” Oiyao giggled. “Haha! This is the best!” Oiyao was overjoyed.

Just then, the door creaked open. Takar walked into the room adjusting her own ribi gaseng. She stopped in her tracks when she saw the toys. “Wow, you look so good! Did you all dress up?” She whispered. “Come on, you’re not staying on the shelf today.&nbsp; You’re joining the celebration.”

“There you go! You’ll have the best seat in the house!” she said with a wink. Oiyao swayed to the music. “This is amazing!” “I love this festival!” Menam said with glee.

“See?” Bangkon said. “The festival may have changed, but its spirit is still strong.” Appun nodded, “As long as people keep sowing seeds and celebrating together,&nbsp; Ali-Aye Ligang will always be special.”

“Takar, your Yam:buks look so lovely!” Ma said. “I know, right, Ma? They are also celebrating!” Takar joyfully exclaimed. “I will bring out some more fabric. Let’s make some new ones for next year,” Ma said. “Yay! More toys!” Takar’s joy knew no bounds.

“Did you hear that? There will be more of us!” Menam cried. “More young ones to celebrate with, that sounds wonderful!” Bangkon beamed. Oiyao grinned, “And now, we have our own festival story to tell them!”

The Ali-Aye Ligang of that year was one that they remembered for a long, long time.

Glossary Yambuk Sonam: Mising folk toy made out of bamboo scraps Yambuk: toys Egg Adin: pork meat Purang apin: sticky rice Apong: rice beer Erpob: scarf Lai hak: leafy vegetable Ribi gaseng: a traditional attire worn by women

About the story Ali-Aye Ligang is the most significant agricultural festival of the Mising (Mishing) tribe of Assam. Celebrated annually on the first Wednesday of the Assamese month of Phagun (February–March), the festival marks the beginning of the sowing season for crops, particularly paddy. Ali means seed, aye means fruits, and ligang means to sow. The festival begins with the eldest member of the family sowing the first seeds in the fields, seeking blessings for a prosperous harvest. Young men and women perform the Gumraag Soman dance to traditional drum beats and bamboo instruments. Special dishes like porag (rice-based food) and fish &amp; pork preparations are enjoyed, along with aapong (homemade rice beer). The younger generation seeks blessings from elders and celebrates the harmony between humans and nature. Ali-Aye Ligang is not just a festival of agriculture but also a time for community bonding, cultural performances, and expressing gratitude to nature.&nbsp; It reflects the Mising people's deep connection with farming, rivers, and forests, making it an essential part of their identity and heritage. Yambuk Sonam is a traditional folk game played by the Mising community, and it serves both as a form of entertainment and a way to pass down stories, skills, and social bonding within the community. Often played during leisure time, it is especially connected to women and children. While women worked in the fields, they would sometimes give folk toys to their children to keep them engaged. These toys weren’t just for play — they also helped teach children about everyday life, responsibilities, and cultural values through imaginative role-playing. So, Yambuk Sonam and the use of toys are both deeply rooted in community life, caregiving, and cultural transmission.
