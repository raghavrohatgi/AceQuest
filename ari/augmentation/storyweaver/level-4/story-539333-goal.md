# Goal!

What? Are those girls playing football? Girls like me? Girls my age?&nbsp; Girls from my Mumbra? Every Sunday, Bhai plays cricket&nbsp; there with his friends. Every Sunday, I help&nbsp; Ammi in the kitchen after&nbsp; I finish my homework.

It’s Sunday again. I tiptoe&nbsp; out of home and run to the&nbsp; playground. “Ummm… do you play football&nbsp; every Sunday?”&nbsp; I ask Didi.

“Yes, we play here every Sunday. Would you like to play with us?” Didi smiles. I shake my head. “Heena!” Bhai roars. “What are you&nbsp; doing here? Go home.” I run home.

Another Sunday. The same team of girls. The same football. The same energy. The same passion. Oh, is that Inayat?

“Inayat!” I call out. “Talk to my mother?” “To let you play football?” Inayat asks, as she kicks the ball.

A few days later. It’s Inayat! She’s come home with her mother, and the same Didi.

“We teach girls football,” Saba Didi says. “How does that help?” Ammi is surprised. They talk. They listen. They talk and listen for one hour. And finally… Ammi and Abbu agree. I can play football!

Sunday again. My heart — it is happy, it is scared. Can everyone hear it? Today is the day, I am finally going to play.&nbsp; I will run.&nbsp; I will kick. But I am also scared. So many people will be watching me.

What if I fall and break my bones?&nbsp; Or if I don’t&nbsp; know how to play football? Ammi and I are at the playground. Bhai and his friends are playing cricket on the other side.

“Heena, just focus on the ball,”&nbsp; Coach says. “Pass it to someone&nbsp; the second it comes to you.&nbsp; Or kick it towards the goalpost.”

I am quiet. The rest of the team is shouting out to each other. “Here, here!” “Ball!” “Ayesha, here! Pass the ball to me,&nbsp; come on.” “Nazo, here,&nbsp; pass, pass.”

Suddenly — The ball is in front of me. I forget everything. Everyone. Now, it is just me. And the football. I kick it hard…

Everyone claps, even Ammi. “Wow, Heena!” “Amazing, Heena!” Bhai? His jaw has dropped to the ground. The ball is out of the ground, but I am in. Oh yes! I am now part of this dream-team.

The times are changing. Can you feel that? People used to say, “How can girls play football?”&nbsp; Now, the same people — friends, neighbours,&nbsp; family members, even strangers — support us. They whistle and cheer for us at our matches.

Now, it feels like there’s a future beyond&nbsp; the kitchen, home and family. Our future, full of possibilities. Our dreams, limitless like the sky.

Simran If not for Parcham, a lot of girls from Mumbra would be at home, preparing for their weddings. Madina When I talk to Abbu, I am motivated because he understands me.&nbsp; He is&nbsp; the best Abbu. Kulsum Earlier, I wanted&nbsp; to&nbsp; be like Ammi.&nbsp; Now,&nbsp; I want to be myself. Mantasha The word I love the most is "to soar".

Jhuveriya I want to tell girls&nbsp; that struggles are&nbsp; a constant. But you&nbsp; must decide&nbsp; what you&nbsp; want to do,&nbsp; what you&nbsp; want to be. Shabnam When I go to the field, I think that today I will learn something and also teach something. Fatima If I were Prime Minister for a day, I would erase hate from the country. Sonali For me, Parcham means&nbsp; joy and freedom.

Is&nbsp; this a true story? In a way Heena's story is true for the girls at Parcham Collective,&nbsp; a&nbsp; non-profit feminist collective in&nbsp; Mumbra, Thane. In 2012, a group&nbsp; of teenage girls and women came&nbsp; together to form the collective&nbsp; and break stereotypes based&nbsp; on&nbsp; religion, caste, class and gender.&nbsp; The collective's name, Parcham,&nbsp; is inspired by Majaz’s Urdu&nbsp; poem and means flag&nbsp; or banner.&nbsp; Parcham Collective wishes&nbsp; to&nbsp; empower young girls to dream of&nbsp; a financially independent future.
