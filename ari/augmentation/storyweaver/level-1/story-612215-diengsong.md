# Diengsong

Whoa! Whoa!&nbsp; The earth is shaking again!

Ah ha ha ha!

…98…99… What comes after 99?

166…167… Uff I know&nbsp; too&nbsp; many numbers.

Hep-hep, do you know how many more numbers you need to count to reach 2000? &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp;&nbsp; &nbsp; Mei!

About 'Diengsong' Diengsong is a small village at the bottom of a hill, in the East Khasi Hills of Meghalaya. One has to descend over 2000 steep steps to reach the village. With a population of 364 people, and 83 houses (as of June 2023 when the TFFP team visited the village), agriculture is one major activities of the village, with broom cultivation being a major part of the villagers' lives. ‘Diengsong’ is a story very close to the author’s heart because of the sheer joy she experienced on her visit to the village. Each page in the story reflects a personal experience that left an impression on the author. The little girl Hep-hep was inspired from the children of the primary school the TFFP team visited. During their visit on a rainy day, there was a tremor in the hill. While the members of the TFFP team got to rush out of the classroom, the children outside danced with the tremors, their laughter enveloping the rain and the fog.&nbsp;&nbsp; These 2000+ steps are integral for every ‘Diengsong-ian.' From matters of birth to death, these steps are deeply intertwined with the villagers' lives. They would have to climb up the hill to access the nearest PHC (Public Health Centre) or to reach the village cemetery at the top.&nbsp; The author hopes that this story embodies the resilience and simplicity of the village.

Glossary Hep-hep:&nbsp; an endearing name usually associated with younger girls in the Khasi tribe of Meghalaya. Mei: a Khasi word for mother.

'Diengsong,' is part of the 45 Meghalaya-contextual storybooks curated under The Forgotten Folklore Project (TFFP), an initiative of the Sauramandala Foundation.&nbsp; The TFFP team is grateful to Mr Teilang Diengdoh and the Diengsong Presbyterian LP School for sharing extensive information on the Diengsong steps and lifestyle of the people,&nbsp; without which this book would not have been possible.
