# Picnic and Pine Needles

"Mmm?" Pa mumbled as he smoked his pipe while mending the tin khat ñiut . "Oh! It seems like the mattress is getting thinner! When can we go out to collect pine needles?" asked Mei.

Bih, Deng, and Hep, without any hesitation, shouted in unison, "This Sngi saitjaiñ, Mei!"

Sngi thohdieng arrived and Mei and Pa returned from work with their&nbsp; pla ïew full of vegetables, dry fish, and some p u nei .

They laid out all the vegetables and other groceries on the low table in the kitchen, sorting and peeling them for next day’s lunch.

" Shooo !!….niii&nbsp; Go to your own home for food!" Deng woke up to the sound of Mei shooing the neighbour’s cat. The delicious smell of dried fish wafted through the whole house.

"Hep, Bih, wake up," Deng said, shaking awake the other two.&nbsp; “It's&nbsp; Sngi saitjaiñ." “It’s picnic time!” Hep giggled.

After a quick breakfast, Mei had already packed their lunches&nbsp; and Pa was ready with the bags.

All of them set out to the bus stop and off they went towards Upper Shillong.

Once they got off the bus, they started walking towards Shillong Peak.&nbsp; There were plenty of small groves that Pa was familiar with.&nbsp; He grew up in Upper Shillong and he knew the place well.&nbsp; Finally, they arrived in one of the groves.

It was a beautiful grove&nbsp; filled with pine trees. Bih turned to Mei and asked, "Mei, these green pine shoots smell so good. Are they also medicinal?" "Yes, of course they are!&nbsp; &nbsp;Most of the plants that grow in these hills are medicinal. We are truly blessed."

Hep immediately tried tasting one of the pine needles. "Phuuuu! Yuck!&nbsp; It tastes like hair." "Shish! not the dry needle you goat, only young green shoots!" said Bih, as they laughed.

The three got to work and started gathering.

"Mei Mei, I am hungry, can we have&nbsp; lunch?" Hep asked. Bih laughed, "Hep, I thought you ate something on the way." "That was just an old NP chewing gum that I left in my pocket, the other day!" Hep cried.

Mei took out her jai ñ kyrshah and spread it on the ground. The delicious food was spread out and everyone gathered around to enjoy the feast. "There is something about the outdoors and the aroma of pine trees that makes your appetite better," Pa remarked. "I just realised just how giving these pine trees are, from their needles, cones, and the bark and timber. I wish we could also be as generous," Mei reflected.

After lunch, all of them went back to collecting the pine needles.&nbsp; After few hours, Pa declared that they had enough and now it was time to pack.

They all started to stuff the piled needles into the burlap bags. &nbsp; Packed and ready, they carried their day's work and headed towards the main road to wait for a bus to take them home.

In the next few days, Mei happily stitches new cotton mattress casings.&nbsp; Bih, Deng, and Hep help fill these casings with the pine needles they collected.

…..And they are ready!&nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp;Oh, how firm and bouncy they are!

At night as they sleep, they dream of more picnics and pines, blissfully surrounded by the sweet, citrusy, woody pine scented mattresses.

Activity &nbsp; There are so many different kinds of pine needles. Would you like to give it a try and draw the different shapes? Take some paper and a pencil and try drawing out your own pine needles.

About ' Picnic and Pine Needles' &nbsp; This is a story based on a very nostalgic childhood experience of the author which left a very special impression on them. It took her several years to fully appreciate and understand the power of family bonding. With the limited nature of time and the fleeting nature of moments experienced, the author reflects back to the nostalgic days of her childhood and remembers them with fondness.&nbsp; This story gives a peek into the old practices of mattress making and other traditional knowledge which were a crucial part of the author's childhood. Through this story, the author wishes to help readers understand that time spent with family is a time that should be prioritised.

Glossary Mei: &nbsp;a Khasi word for mother Pa: father Tin khat-ñiut: dust pan Bih: general nickname for a girl Deng: general nickname for a sibling who is a middle child Hep: a general nickname for a younger sibling Sngi saitjaiñ: &nbsp;A day for washing clothes or doing laundry/ Saturday Sngi thohdieng: A day to collect fire wood for the kitchen/ Friday Pla-ïew: a cloth bag usually used to carry to the market Pu-nei: a rice based pancake made with perilla seed paste Nii: an expression of being annoyed or angry
