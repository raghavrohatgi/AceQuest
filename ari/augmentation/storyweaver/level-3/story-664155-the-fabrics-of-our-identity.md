# The Fabrics of Our Identity

“The motifs of Assamese attire are nature's whispers—reminding us to observe, to belong to this land, and to preserve it with care.” Biswajit Thakuria Assamese, Assam

“They travelled with me Off we went to Cultural Sundays Off we went to NSF programmes Off we went to our Festivals Now they sleep in my closet until one of those days come.” Niboli K Chophimi Sumi-Naga Tribe, Nagaland

“Traditional garments carry stories through generations, passed down with memories. After my grandma passed away, my aunt gave us some of Atsa’s favorite pieces—a piece of her woven into our lives.” Chorovelu Ringa Chakhesang Tribe and Angami Tribe, Nagaland

“Watching my Ane weave Gale all day while sitting/sleeping beside her is one of the best feelings I still cherish.” Marba Kamki Galo Tribe, Arunachal Pradesh

“‘Puanchei’ and ‘Kawrchei’, traditional Mizo attire, tell stories of our heritage. More than just a dress, it's a symbol of unity that weaves together the hearts of the Mizo people.” Joan lalramngaihsangi Lusei Mizo, Mizoram

“I simply love to wear the red and white luirim shawl. It invariably gives me a sense of pride as a native Tangkhul and a motivation to go on in life.” H.A Sothing Shimray Tangkhul Tribe, Manipur

“These traditional shawls for me represent love, warmth and home.” Kemya Yanlem Konyak Tribe &amp; Tangkhul Tribe, Nagaland

“Tucked away and only worn for occasions makes the garments feel extra special when worn.” Keneisenuo Solo Angami Tribe &amp; Tangkhul Tribe, Nagaland

“To me, my Indigenous cultural attire and wearing it, is more than just normal outfits – It carries epistemic living, knowing and being– it is a living identity and a covert resistance, with its each thread, lines and designs carries the pride, distinctness, and beauty of my community.” Thomas Malsom Hwrangkhawl Tribe &amp; Malsom Tribe, Tripura

“I truly cherish how our traditional attire feels during special occasions. No matter the weather, the soft silk shawl always provides warmth, even when paired with just a simple top. The lower garment, the phanek, is more than just clothing; it carries a sense of empowerment. Growing up, we were told stories of legendary women like Queen Linthoingambi, who went to war wearing a phanek, and Princess Thoibi, who rode a horse in one. As a writer, I’ve always enjoyed attending events dressed in the vibrant hues of phanek, whether in a simple design or the more elegant Mayek Naibi—each shade and style tells its own story.” Potsangbam Linthoingambi Chanu Meitei Tribe, Manipur

“As a child, my sister and I would practice draping the mekhela sador weeks before Bihu and yet would fumble for Maa's help on the day of the occasion simply because we felt more confident when maa made the pleats. Alot of my love for the attire comes from the tenderness with which maa treated the cloth. Maa's tenderness for the cloth eventually translated to her carefully embroidering little flowers all throughout the edge of my first very own Sador, which I have now outgrown but continues to warm me.” Kasturi Dev Choudhury Assamese, Assam

“This is the traditional attire of the Galo tribe worn during their main festival, Mopin.” Moge Basar Galo Tribe, Arunachal Pradesh

“This bakhu is very special as my Sano Gaga (Grandmother) bought it for me last Losar. It has my favourite colour pink.” Tashi Chong Sherpa Sherpa Tribe, Sikkim

“Every time I wear my dokhona it feels like a challenge that I have accomplished because of the way it is worn and that nothing can beat the feel of that panache.” Chalini Basumatary Bodo Tribe, Aassam

“Wearing my traditional attire is one of the ways to show my love and pride for my identity and culture.” Longang Luklem Konyak Tribe, Nagaland
