# Bei Rym-aw

“What are we going to wear to the festival, Waka?” Kiru asks.

“ I will be wearing my muka and thoh saru !” says Waka. “I also want one! I also want one!”&nbsp; Kiru jumps up in excitement.

Waka laughs and says, “Oh, Kiru! I’m so sorry but they don’t make attires for squirrels.” Kiru looks up at Waka with a pout. “But I want to look nice for my first Chad Sukra .” “What if I make you a matching turban? It'll suit you perfectly!”

The day of the festival, Waka and Kiru excitedly run up to the festival grounds, all decked out in their new attires. Kiru is bouncing around, trying to take all the festivities in. “Wow, there is just sooo much to see!” Kiru cries out.

“That man is called a Daloi. He is praying for a bountiful harvest” explains Waka to a curious little Kiru. “Is that why we celebrate this festival?” Kiru asks.

“Yes, the Chad Sukra is a festival where we pray for good crops and blessings from Bei Rym-aw !" “Is it celebrated only by the Pnar people?” Kiru asks. “Yes, Kiru. The Chad Sukra is a special bond between the Pnar people and Bei Rym-aw .”

As they move further, Waka and Kiru come across people dancing with plates. Kiru's eyebrows furrow in confusion. "So, why do they use plates during their dance?" Waka chuckles, "The plates are part of Ka Chad Pliang dance, showing how we all come together as one.”

Strolling through the bustling grounds, they spot a lone figure in the corner. “Waka, do you see that Kong?” Kiru asks. &nbsp;“Yes, Kiru. I wonder why she looks so sad?&nbsp; Let's go and see if she needs any help.”

The two slowly approach the lonely figure wrapped in tattered rags. “Hi, Kong. Are you looking for someone?” “Hello, little ones.&nbsp; I didn’t realise&nbsp; people could still see me.” “What do you mean?”

The old lady sighs softly before revealing her disfigured body. “I am not just a Kong. I am who they call Bei Rym-aw herself. I’ve watched over the land and its creatures for long, but I’ve grown tired now.” “My beloved body does not feel like my own anymore,” says Bei Rym-aw to a bewildered Waka and Kiru.

“But…. but, you are worshipped every year,” Waka mumbles sadly. Letting out a sigh,&nbsp; Bei Rym-aw says, “Humans worship me, but they have also failed me. The air, the land, and the water are all polluted, making me weak.”

Their shoes go crunch-crunch on plastic wrappers scattered on the ground, making Waka frown. After the encounter with Bei Rym-aw , Waka and Kiru stroll through the festival feeling a bit sad.

She suddenly stops walking. “Kiru, do you want to do some cleanup today?” Kiru chuckles, “Like the nature cleanup crew?” “Exactly! And you're my trusty sidekick.” Kiru strikes a pose. “The Super Squirrel Cleaner-upper, reporting for duty!” Waka giggles and they start cleaning up the ground.

Holding up a shiny wrapper, Kiru says, “Waka, look what I found! So shiny and crinkly, it's like squirrel treasure!” Waka smiles. “It might look cool, but it's not good for Bei Rym-aw . We can put it in one khoh with the other dry waste.” “And what about the banana peel?” “Ah, this will go in the khoh for wet waste.” Kiru nods and says, “Aye! Aye! Captain!”

After cleaning up for a while, they decide to take a break. “Look at that, Waka. Our ground looks so much better already.” Waka smiles warmly.&nbsp; “It sure does, Kiru.”

“Do you think this will help Bei Rym-aw feel a little better?”

Activity Join the nature cleanup crew!&nbsp; Find things like wrappers and plastic bags and collect them in one basket/bag. Then, look for things like food scraps, fruit peels and put them in another basket/bag, just like Waka and Kiru did.

Glossary Chad Sukra: This is a festival that honours Bei Rym-aw for bountiful blessings. Celebrated in the month of April, it signals the beginning of sowing season. Daloi:&nbsp; ﻿a priest. Kong : a salutation for an older woman in Pnar. Bei Rym-aw : symbolizes Mother Nature in the Jaiñtia culture. Khoh : refers to a traditional bamboo basket used for various purposes.
