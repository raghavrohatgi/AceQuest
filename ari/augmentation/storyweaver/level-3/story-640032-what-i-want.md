# What I Want

Assert I want to be heard—to voice my thoughts and be understood. Japani Shyam, Gond artist

Pl ay I want to laugh and explore the world without fear. Kamta Tahed, Pithora artist

Celebrate I want the solidarity of female friendships, where we can rely on each other. Minakshi Vayeda, Warli artist

Protect I want to conserve nature, as I draw strength&nbsp; from the trees around me. Lado Bai, Bhil artist

ABOUT THE ART FORMS AND THE ARTISTS Six indigenous artists came together to create a book about their hopes and dreams. Not only for themselves, but for girls everywhere. Together, they drew a brighter, more equitable future. PATTACHITRA Odisha Pattachitra paintings, which are painted on cloth in Odisha and West Bengal, are inspired by mythology and are known for their delicate and detailed works.&nbsp; Gitanjali Das is a Pattachitra artist from Odisha. She started painting at a young age, first learning from her father, then in partnership with her late husband, the Pattachitra artist Pranab Das. Gitanjali has participated in many workshops and exhibitions across the country. GOND ART Practised by the Pardhan Gonds of Madhya Pradesh, this tradition draws on myths and folklore inspired by nature. It is full of delicate motifs, intricate designs and bright colours.&nbsp; &nbsp; Japani Shyam is a contemporary Gond painter based in Bhopal. Japani’s experimental work reflects the stories of the forest. Her works have been displayed in exhibitions and at galleries across India.

PITHORA ART Drawing from the myths and folklore of the indigenous tribes of central and western India, Pithora art is characterised by its bright colours and the dots or tipna that cover the paintings.&nbsp; Kamta Tahed is a Pithora artist. She started painting at a young age, inspired by the women around her, but was able to pursue art full-time only much later in life. She has participated in art exhibitions across the country and won prestigious awards. BHIL ART Stories of the land and the human-nature relationship are the foundation of the legends and folklore of the Bhil tribe in central and western India. Bhil art features bright colours and an overlay of intricate patterns of dots.&nbsp; Born in Madhya Pradesh, Lado Bai is a Bhil artist. Lado Bai’s works are steeped in spirituality and animism and reflect the flora and fauna of her environment, along with Bhil rituals and festivals. A multiple award-winning artist, her works have been displayed at art festivals and in museums.

PHAD Artists from the Bhopa community of Rajasthan paint on a cloth scroll called a phad. The art tells the stories of folk heroes.&nbsp; Kritika Joshi is a 30th-generation Phad artist. A graduate of the Indian Institute of Crafts and Design in Jaipur, Kritika has mastered the art of Phad painting and actively engages with private and non-profit organisations to promote the craft. The paintings by the Warli tribe of Maharashtra depict everyday life and nature using simple geometrical shapes like triangles and circles, and vivid colours.&nbsp; Minakshi Vayeda grew up watching women Warli artists paint during weddings, birth ceremonies and other celebrations, and was encouraged by her father to practise the art form. From painting on walls of houses, which is the traditional canvas of Warli, Minakshi now paints on cloth and paper and has participated in exhibitions around India. WARLI ART
