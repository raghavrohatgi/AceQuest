# The Fishing Trip

Thoni had a plan. “I will catch so many fish today.”

“Nu, I want to eat&nbsp; fish curry! Take me&nbsp; fishing.” “Not today, Thoni,” Nu replied.

But Thoni wanted to eat fish curry today. “I will go by myself!”

“Thoni, where are you going?”&nbsp; asked Niffu. “I am going fishing for fish&nbsp; curry!”

“Fishing?&nbsp; Do you even know&nbsp; how to fish?” “You will see!”

“Bubu, Come with us!”, Thoni and Niffu called out.

“But fishing will be fun! Do you not want to eat&nbsp; a&nbsp; delicious fish curry?” “No, no, I cannot” said Bubu. “If I am late, my Nu will scold me.”

“Delicious fish…” “Lots of delicious fish!”

“Fishing is not as easy as it&nbsp; looks.” “Do not worry, you will catch some fish, Thoni.” “This isn’t fair!”

*Rustle Rustle* Who? What?

Oh! Oh No! Huh!

“OH. It is just foolish&nbsp; Jamdai!”

“Huh! I have seen my&nbsp; mother fishing many&nbsp; times. I know exactly how&nbsp; to catch fish!”&nbsp; Thoni retorted. “Trying to catch some fish, I&nbsp; see. But you are not doing it&nbsp; right.”

“Really? Then show me the fish you have caught?”

“Haha, why don't I show you&nbsp; a better way&nbsp; to catch fish?&nbsp; With a little magic of mine.&nbsp; Now, calm your tempers and watch&nbsp; carefully” “Oh, and give me a&nbsp; snack, please”

“Hmmmm... nom... nom... nom... fish, appear, fish, appear.” “Get ready to catch some fish!”

“Whoa! Look at them, let us catch&nbsp;them all!” “So many fish!”

“STOP! Take only what you need. Leave the rest” “But why?”

“If we take everything today, there will be&nbsp; none left for tomorrow” “But why?”

“It is really not magic, you see. I only used some roots to make the fish a little drowsy. This makes them float on the surface so we may catch them easily.” “Jamdai, what is the secret behind your magic trick?”

“But would it not be easier to use an&nbsp; electric machine than some leaf or root?” “My uncle uses a battery made electric&nbsp; machine to catch a lot of fish, as it shocks&nbsp; them” “It is true, you can catch&nbsp; a lot of fish and catch&nbsp; them quickly with the&nbsp; machine…”

“But the machine kills the fishes, their eggs, and harms them before they even have a chance to grow” “It is important to think about the future as well” “Hmmmmm” “That’s so sad.” “You are right.”

“Bye Bye Jamdai. Thank you!” “Go on, now.&nbsp; It is&nbsp; getting late”

“How did you catch so many fishes?” “Nu we are back!” “Come along! I will make some fish curry.” “We had some help,” Thoni replied.

And they all enjoyed the delicious fish curry! “ Thank you Jamdai!&nbsp; ” the three children silently whispered

Glossary • Nu: The Hwrangkhawl word for mother • Ralai/Bailing: A bamboo made tool used to clean crude rice by removing husk dust • Sudam: A bamboo made fishing gear/tool use to catch the fish in the river • Nga-Phoum: An Indigenous fish dish • Nga-Tung: An Indigenous fish curry dish that is cooked inside Bamboo • See-Phut: A millet-based sweet snack made with jaggary • Battery-made electric machine: A harmful modern tool used to electrocute fish,&nbsp; &nbsp; damaging aquatic ecosystems

About the story Jamdai: Jamdai, known both as a person and a mythical figure, holds significant importance in the cultural life of the Hrangkhawl community, one of the indigenous communities residing in state of Tripura. Jamdai as a character is highly rooted in the folktales of the Hangkhawls, with numerous stories centered around him. There is a popular saying that every Jamdai story does not have a beginning or an end. Although, Jamdai is often portrayed as dumb, lazy, and foolish, he is known for his humorous nature and unique way of living and being, which sets him apart from regular humans. Despite his perceived flaws, this character is culturally and historically significant to the Hrangkhawl community. Roo-Deng: Roo-Deng is the fishing tradition practice by the Hrangkhawl community. Roo is the name the plant used by the people of the community to catch fish. It is scientifically called Florida Fishpoison Tree ( Piscidia piscipula ). It is found in many parts of the world, commonly used by people from indigenous communities for its natural piscicidal properties. The native or indigenous tribes like Hrangkhawls of Tripura use extracts from its bark, roots, twigs, and leaves, to sedate fish that make it easier or simpler to catch them. This practice capitalizes on the toxic compounds present in the tree, primarily rotenone, which affects aquatic organisms by interfering with cellular respiration. The tree’s common name reflects its historical and cultural association with this traditional fishing technique which allows people from the community to catch fish without harming the overall ecosystem.
