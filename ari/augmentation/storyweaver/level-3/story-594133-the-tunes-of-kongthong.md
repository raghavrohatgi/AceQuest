# The Tunes of Kongthong

It is a bright and pleasant day at Kongthong.&nbsp; Kyrpang is excited and&nbsp; eagerly awaits the&nbsp; arrival of his friends to go on a picnic with&nbsp; them.

“Bye, Mei!” Kyrpang calls out to his mother as he runs with&nbsp; his friends.&nbsp; Shida smiles as she looks at him and calls out,&nbsp; “Don’t be late, Kyrpang!”

Kyrpang crosses the root bridge with his friends Shemphang and Pyndap.&nbsp; Discussing the adventure that lies ahead, Kyrpang says, “since this picnic route is so beautiful, I wonder how lovely our picnic will be!”

“We are finally here!” Krypang says, while Shemphang and Pyndap excitedly jump into the stream and say in unison, “We haven’t played in this stream for a long time!”

After playing for sometime, Shemphang decides&nbsp; to search for shiny water stones nearby when&nbsp; he suddenly notices something.&nbsp; He points towards&nbsp; a small cave next to the waterfall, “Look!&nbsp; I have never seen that before!”

Once they get inside the cave, Pyndap is amazed to see white fishes.&nbsp; “Wow! Look,&nbsp; fishes!” he points.&nbsp;&nbsp; He is followed by Shemphang who suddenly&nbsp;shrieks in excitement,&nbsp; “A crab too!”&nbsp; Enjoying their outing, the boys lose track of time while playing…

…when they suddenly realise that they have lost&nbsp; &nbsp;their way.

Shemphang, looking worried,&nbsp; remarks, “I think we came&nbsp; this way!” to which&nbsp;Pyndap&nbsp; replies,&nbsp; “No, it was this way!”

I n the midst of the confusion, the boys hear a&nbsp; familiar tune coming from outside the cave…

They listen intently to the tune as Kyrpang cries out, “Listen, I think I hear someone!”&nbsp; Pyndap, looking relieved adds, “someone is nearby!”

Kyrpang responds to the familiar tune&nbsp; by&nbsp; humming his own tune, while his friends look&nbsp; on…hopeful for a&nbsp; response.

Suddenly, Krypang hears his own tune being hummed back.&nbsp; &nbsp; “I think someone is&nbsp; very close&nbsp; now,” he says, eagerly&nbsp; looking towards the direction of the sound.&nbsp; Shempang and Pyndap look in&nbsp; the same&nbsp; direction&nbsp; cheerfully.

The boys then slowly make their way towards the tune to find &nbsp;Kyrpang’s Uncle emerge&nbsp; before them. “It’s you boys!&nbsp; Why are you&nbsp; still&nbsp; out here?”&nbsp; says the&nbsp;uncle,&nbsp; shocked to see&nbsp;&nbsp; them&nbsp; out in the&nbsp;forest late&nbsp; in the evening,

As they walk back towards the village, Kyrpang’s uncle tells them,&nbsp; “Your voice is like&nbsp; the&nbsp; sound&nbsp;of the river nymphs. My grandmother used to tell me stories about nymphs staying&nbsp; in the&nbsp;&nbsp;caves&nbsp;and the rivers singing beautiful melodies. I’m glad you sang your&nbsp;&nbsp; tune and I&nbsp; heard you&nbsp;calling.”

“Mei!” cries out Kyrpang as he sees his worried mother&nbsp; waiting for him.

“Your son got lost in a cave near the waterfall. Luckily I heard him singing&nbsp; his tune,” Kyrpang’s uncle tells&nbsp; Mei. “I told you not to be late,” Mei&nbsp; reprimands Kyrpang.&nbsp;

She then embraces him.&nbsp; Kyrpang hugs his mother back and tells her,&nbsp; “If not for Kongthong’s tunes, my friends&nbsp; and I would have lost our way today!”&nbsp; Shida hums her son’s tune and says, “Our tunes are always there to help us. That is why we sing.&nbsp; They are songs inspired by our ancestral mother – our clan mother. She always protects us. Never forget that and never forget your tunes – your ‘ Jingrwai Iawbei ,’&nbsp; the tune of Kongthong. "

“I think I understand Mei.&nbsp; I want to&nbsp; call out to my friends too, we had&nbsp;such&nbsp; a tough day together!” says Kyrpang.

Jingrwai Iawbei It is tradition in Kongthong for mothers to compose tunes for their children. The tunes are their unofficial names! Every member of the village has a tune composed by their parents. It is called the ‘Jingrwai Iawbei’ .&nbsp; Everyone has their own unique tune, which is sung by others, while calling them, or sung by the person to whom the tune belongs, to inform others that they are nearby. The members of the community are said to be able to recognise each other’s tunes making it the preferred method of calling people.

Activity Would you like to have a tune as your name? How about you try composing a short tune for your loved ones, similar to&nbsp;how the people in Kongthong create melodies for each other?

Glossary J ingrwai Iawbei: &nbsp;roughly translates to ‘song of the ancestral mother,’ is a cultural belief practised at Khatarshnong and adjoining areas of the Khasi Hills where parents, mainly mothers, create tunes for their children. These tunes serve as their unofficial names that are recognisable within their cultural context. It is believed that such tunes are inspired by the ancestral maternal mother of respective clans and no two individuals share the same tune. Jai ñ kyrshah : &nbsp;a traditional attire of the Khasi tribe of Meghalaya worn by women. Shida, Krypang's mother, is shown wearing this in the story. It also serves the purposes of an apron. Mei: &nbsp;a Khasi word for mother. Kyrpang:&nbsp; means ‘special’ in the Khasi language. It is also the name of one of the boys in the story. Pyndap: &nbsp;means ‘fulfilment’ in Khasi. It is also the name of one of the boys in the story. Shemphang: &nbsp;means ‘wisdom’ in Khasi. It is also the name of one of the boys in the story. Mewanshan: &nbsp;means ‘to come support’ in Khasi. It is also the name of the uncle in the story.
