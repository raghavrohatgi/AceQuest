# Khampai Becomes a Yaopa

“Remember, Khampai, to become perfect, one has to overcome the fear of making mistakes.”

“I wish you were here, Apu.”

“Apu, are you making yanshahbo again?” “Yes, I am. Come, I'll show you how it's made.”

“This is nyahrih.” “When it is hot, it turns all soft and squishy, and you can shape it however you want.”

“Remember, Khampai, to become perfect, one has to overcome the fear of making mistakes.” “My hands are experienced, because they have made enough mistakes.” “Remember, Khampai, to become perfect, one has to overcome the fear of making mistakes.”

“One has to overcome the fear of making mistakes.”

“Being a Yaopa is a privilege. I do this work with love for the people and the art. “You know, Khampai, Longwa is one of the only few villages where this art is still alive. It connects us with the rest of the Konyaks.”

“Apu, how does nyahrih become a golden metal?” “Nyahrih won’t turn into metal. However, it will help make the mould for brass casting.” “Once the design cools and hardens, we will cover it with clay formed from a mixture of sand aggregate and ash…

…then, we will bury it under the charcoal to heat.”

“Apu, where is the ball I put inside!!?” “Hahaha, the fire ate it away. But don’t worry, it left behind the shape we need.”

“We take the brass scraps and put them in the pot to melt them.” “Once the brass scraps melt, we will pour the molten brass into the moulds to fill its hollow cavities.”

“Move away further Khampai... ...it can be dangerous to be too close.”

“We’ll let the brass cool down and solidify before we break it.” “We’ll let the brass cool down and solidify before we break it.”

“Tap the mould gently with the hammer to loosen the sand.” “What if I break it, Apu?” “Don’t worry, you won’t break it. Just gently hammer it open.”

“It’s there, Apu!&nbsp; My marble has turned go lden.&nbsp; It’s magic! You are a magician!” “The show isn’t over yet. The edges need to be smooth.”

“Now the show is complete.”

“I miss you so much, Apu.”

“I hope I can keep your magic alive.”

Glossary Apu: A Longwa word for grandfather. Yanshahbo: A traditional necklace worn by Konyak men, particularly warriors. ‘Yanshahbo’ is a Longwa term referring to the necklace&nbsp; adorned with replicas of human heads. The number of heads is&nbsp; supposed to symbolize the number of enemy heads taken by the&nbsp; wearer. Nyahrih: Blackened beeswax. It is softened and shaped&nbsp; into a desired design, either by hand or using moulds. Yaopa: In this story it refers to a brass worker.

About the Story&nbsp; ‘Yaopa’ means ‘craftsman’ in the Longwa language. Longwa is a&nbsp;Konyak village in the Mon district of Nagaland. It lies on the&nbsp;international border between India and Myanmar and is uniquely positioned such that one part of it lies in India while the other part&nbsp;lies in Myanmar. The story is set in Longwa. Known for their skilled craftsmanship, it is one of the few Konyak villages where traditional brass necklaces are still made. The traditional necklaces for Konyak men, called ‘Yanshahbo’ in the Longwa language, are worn by Konyak warriors to indicate the number of enemy heads they have taken. Headhunting was a common practice among the Konyak tribe as it was deem necessary for a village to produce strong warriors in order to survive the enemy raids and to strongly make its presence felt.

While this practice came to an end with the influence of Christianity in the region and the government ban on it, the symbol of the heads remained as an essential part of the culture. The necklace is made of brass and features replicas of human heads and a string of beads. The traditional brass workers of Longwa meet the demand for necklaces from the Konyaks of both India and Myanmar. Additionally, they may also receive orders from the Wancho tribe of Arunachal Pradesh, whose traditional necklaces are similar to those of the Konyaks. Thus, the brass workers of Longwa play a significant role, not just for &nbsp; Longwa but for the Konyak community as a whole.
