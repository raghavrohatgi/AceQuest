# When the Clouds Came Home

Dark clouds gather in the sky.&nbsp; Lightning strikes. Dadaji says, “It looks like it is going to rain.”&nbsp; Children dance and sing, waiting for the rains.

Raindrops begin to fall. Trees sway, birds flap their wings and fly home. The animals run helter-skelter. The parched soil is soaked. The farmers pick up their ploughs. It is time to plant. It is time to sow.

The rain lashes down. The streams, the lakes and the rivers overflow. The village is lush. It is green. The farmers go to the fields, the fisherfolk to the river. The peacock dances joyfully.

The rice is ripe. The rice is gold. The farmers go to the fields, sickle in hand. To cut. To gather. To harvest. Some take the grains home to cook. Some store it for another time. It is time to feast.

Everyone is busy.&nbsp; Everyone is happy. The village is full of life. A story as old as the hills.

The village grows and changes, bit by bit. The villagers have to go to the forests dark and deep. First to gather the twigs, then the sticks, then the branches. To cook. To build homes.

People need space.&nbsp; They need more. More of everything.

Contractors enter the village. Trucks carry away the trees. The forest grows smaller. It is quieter. There are fewer animals. The birds don’t sing as much.

Things are changing fast. The village is now a town. There is noise and there is smoke.

The hills lie bare. The trees have disappeared. There is no rain. The streams stop flowing. The villagers now have to walk for miles to get water.

The villagers wait for the rains. They look up, searching for rain clouds. But there’s only the sun. Blazing mighty and fierce, across a dusty sky. The few animals and birds are thirsty, looking for water. &nbsp; The rivers have dried up. The farmers sit silent.&nbsp; They are angry.

“We need trees. We need hills.” Young and old, everyone begins planting trees. The children help dig the soil. They plant the saplings.

Slowly, the hills are lush, they are green. Rain clouds are spotted in the sky. The birds and animals return home. The farmers are ready to go back to the fields. &nbsp; The fisherfolk, to the river. Like the peacock, they all dance with joy.

A VILLAGE BY THE HILL This is the story of Bapugaon, Ramesh Laxman Hengadi’s village, which is nestled amidst the hills and valleys of Dahanu in Maharashtra. Ramesh is a Warli artist. &nbsp;Warli painting depicts Nature, and the artists respect and worship Nature—the sun, sky, water, air and mountains are revered as gods. Bapugaon is home to Adivasis who depend on forests, rivers and farming. But now, their way of life is being put at risk by development projects such as overtourism, ports, railway lines and roads, that do not consider their needs. At the same time, climate change is making life harder. Dangerous weather patterns, rising temperatures and unpredictable rainfall affect farming and their livelihoods. But together, Ramesh and his friends and family in Bapugaon believe things can change for the better.
