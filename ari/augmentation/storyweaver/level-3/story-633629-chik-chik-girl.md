# Chik-chik Girl

“Amma, why do you make such delicious khichdi?” says Mona. Amma’s khichdi has carrots and tomatoes, peas and potatoes, and a few drops of homemade ghee. “Why, Mona?” Amma asks.

“It’s not fair!” says Mona. “It’s too tasty, Amma. I can’t stop eating.” “Don’t complain, then, Mona! No chik-chik!” laughs Amma. “Take a little more.”

“Bedtime, Mona,” says Papa. “Time to put away your storybook.” “No, please, Papa,” says Mona. “My book is so exciting.” “What’s the story about?” he asks. “Five children, and their dog. They are detectives. They are&nbsp; cleverer than the policeman.” “Wake up early and read,” says Papa. “It’s not fair!” grumbles Mona. “Why do people write exciting books I can’t stop reading?”&nbsp; Papa laughs. “Tell me, who reads books they want to stop reading?”

Dadi hums a song as she massages Mona’s hair with&nbsp; her secret recipe of mustard oil. Sometimes Dadi’s fingers feel like she’s playing a tabla, dham-ta-dham on her head. Other times her fingers&nbsp; feel like raindrops. “Will my hair grow quickly, Dadi?” Mona asks. “Yes. Your hair will be soft and long.” “How long?” “Down to your knees,” says Dadi. “As long as mine.”

“It’s not fair, Dadi,” says Mona.&nbsp; “How will&nbsp; my friends’ hair grow strong and long?&nbsp; They don’t have your&nbsp; magic recipe.” “‘Not fair, not fair!’ Always doing chik-chik, chik-chik!” says Dadi.

Dada and Mona are walking back from the market. “Dada, it’s not fair! You walk so fast, I can’t keep up with you,” says Mona.

“If you climb the trees in the back garden, go swimming, drink milk, and eat paalak, daal,&nbsp; and fruits…” “I will grow up big and strong, I know,”&nbsp; says Mona. “… you will soon walk faster than I do.” “One day my legs will be as long as yours, Dada!”&nbsp; says Mona. “Yes, they will.” Dada smiles and ruffles her hair.

“Tee-tee-ti, teeti-ti.” Mona watches the titeeri bird sitting on&nbsp; a branch of the tree outside the window.&nbsp; “Tee-ti, tee-ti, teeti-tee-ti,” replies Mona. A koyal calls out to Mona: “Oo-ee-u, oo-ee-yo.” “Oo-ee-u, oo-ee-yo, koyal!” says Mona. “Did you know we had a koyal in our house?”&nbsp; Dadi asks Amma.

Mona swoops around the house singing, “Oo-ee-u, oo-ee-yo, oo-ee-u, oo-ee-yo.”

“Chee-chee, chew-chee, chrrr. Chee-chee, chew-chee, chrrr…”&nbsp; “How did so many sparrows fly into our house?” says Dada. “Whee-ta-whee-yu, whee-ta-whee-yu, whee-ta-whee-yu,” sings Mona. “The bulbul in our house sings better than&nbsp; the bulbul outside, doesn’t she?” says Amma. “Shh,” says Mona. “The birds will cry if they hear you.&nbsp; It’s not fair if I sing better than a real bulbul, is it?” “We have a chik-chik bird in our house,” says Dada, laughing.

At dinner, Amma says, “Did you know, cooking tasty khichdi is not fair?” “And it’s not fair for anyone to write exciting books,”&nbsp; says Papa. “Or to sing as sweetly as a bird,” Amma goes on. “Or to have long hair,” says Dadi. “Or to have long legs,” says Dada,&nbsp; stretching his legs out under the table.

“My Amma makes the best khichdi,” says Mona. “My Papa&nbsp; brings exciting books for me to read. My Dada has the longest legs, and my Dadi’s hair oil is a magic potion! I am the luckiest&nbsp; girl in the world.”

“Not fair!” says Amma. “Oh, not fair!” says Papa. “That’s not fair!” says Dada. “Tch-tch, so unfair!” says Dadi with a sniff. “You are all doing chik-chik now,” says Mona. “Yes!” Amma, Papa, Dadi and Dada say together. “We are a chik-chik family!”
