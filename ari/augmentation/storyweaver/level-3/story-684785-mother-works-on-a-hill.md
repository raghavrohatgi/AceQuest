# Mother Works on a Hill

My name is Esther. I study in Standard II. My mother’s name is Kumudini Bara. She is a nurse and works in a village on a hill. My mother has told me that nurses like her are called ANM—Auxiliary Nurse Midwife.

When I get ready to go to school, I see that my mother is not at home. Why? Because my mother has already left for work. &nbsp; I ask my mother whenever she is around: “Ayo, where do you work? I want to see where you work. Take me to your office.”

One December, Mother takes me to work with her. I wear a yellow frock with red flowers and my favourite hoodie. Mother wears her clean, white uniform, a navy blue cardigan and a batik scarf.

I carry my favourite backpack with a bottle of water and lots of biscuits and potato chips. Mother carries her huge bag. In it, there is a machine to measure blood pressure. Another machine to measure blood sugar. Another machine to measure haemoglobin. And lots of medicines. And a stethoscope, too.

We take a bus, which drops us at a village at the base of a hill. Mother says that the village she works at is on that hill. Someone is waiting for us at the bus stop.&nbsp; Mother tells&nbsp; me that her name is Salgi Purty.&nbsp; I call her Salgi Aunty. She is a Sahiya, the local healthcare worker. Salgi Aunty has come with her nephew whom I call Jado Bhaiya.&nbsp; Salgi Aunty is carrying a blue box that looks like&nbsp; a huge Ludo die.&nbsp; I wonder what it is.

We begin climbing the hill. There is no road. Just a track cut by the villagers. Only bicycles and motorcycles can go up the hill. We have neither, so we trudge. “During the rains, this track becomes a river,” says Salgi Aunty. “But your mother still comes, and we climb the hill&nbsp; together, carefully.”

The hills are pretty. There are trees like sal, jamun, palash and karanj. There are lovely flowers too, like putus, kudrum and posta. There is a stream where people gather water. We pass villagers carrying big logs of wood on their shoulders and walking down the hill. Finally, we reach the top of the hill, and I can see the village!

“Maiya Esther, today is Thursday,” Mother tells me, “On Thursdays, children and pregnant women are given vaccines.” Now I know what that big blue box is that Salgi Aunty is carrying. It is a vaccine carrier! “The older carriers were small and easy to carry up the hill,” Salgi Aunty says. “These big ones are difficult. But what to do?” She laughs.

Mother begins vaccinating the children and pregnant women. Some children cry as they are jabbed. But Mother takes a toffee from her bag and gives it to the crying child, who immediately stops crying. I did not know Mother carried toffees in her bag! Mother then checks the blood pressure, blood sugar,&nbsp; and haemoglobin of the pregnant women. She checks the villagers who are ill, and gives them medicines.

After some time, I get bored. I join some children who are playing.

At the end of the day, Salgi Aunty and Jado Bhaiya&nbsp; accompany us down the hill.&nbsp; Salgi Aunty gifts me a bag full of kudrum,&nbsp; which Mother&nbsp; will cook into&nbsp; a tart chutney. Jado Bhaiya takes the vaccine carrier to th e auto rickshaw that has come to collect it. Salgi Aunty flags down a bus and sees us off.

I have finally seen where my mother works. I am happy. I am proud of my mother.
