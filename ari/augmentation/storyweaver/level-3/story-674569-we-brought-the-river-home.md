# We brought the river home

Every night, Geetu dreams of water.

How it goes splash when she jumps into the pond near her home. How it sloshes in her bottle as she walks to school. How it babbles when the river comes to life. Water is her friend.

But they don’t get to play much these days. It’s been a long summer—like last year, and the year before, and the year before that. These days, when Geetu races her brother Tipu to the big well, to throw pebbles in, all they hear is a small plop.

The river barely speaks. The pond is nearly dry. &nbsp;

Most of the men have left for the big city to find work. “Enough is enough,” says Maa, in her this-can’t-go-on voice. For her, water is work. When Geetu and Tipu sleep, Maa makes her way to the&nbsp; landlord’s fields at the break of dawn. She walks barefoot, over rocky ground. Because the people in&nbsp; that big house get angry if Maa—or anyone in Geetu’s family—&nbsp; wears slippers in front of them.&nbsp; They are unfair like that.

She fills five pots and two buckets at the hand pump. The one near home has run out of water.

“This can’t go on,” says Kiran Chachi, in her let’s-fix-it voice. Kiran Chachi walks even farther than Maa to bring water to her home. And then her husband, Atul Chacha, gets upset if she can’t make him rotis on time.

“But what can you women do?” scoffs the sarpanch. “Make a river?”

Maa and Kiran Chachi get going. They meet Asha Mausi and Renu Didi and Meera Mausi. They talk and talk and talk some more.&nbsp; The quiet ones and the loud ones, the brave ones and the&nbsp; nervous ones. “But what can we women do?” asks one of them.

“Idea!” exclaims Kiran Chachi. “Let’s bring the river home.” The next morning, as they gather near the dry pond, the women see what Kiran Chachi meant.

Behind the pond is a hillock, and behind it is a river. It is still flowing and still gurgling. But it is&nbsp; going away from the pond and the village.&nbsp; If only they could cut the hill and make a path for it,&nbsp; the water would find its way to their pond.

“But what can women do?” laughs a man under the peepul tree, as he plays cards with his&nbsp; friends.&nbsp; The other men laugh some more. The women ignore them.

They meet the panchayat elders, they meet the government officials.&nbsp; They talk and talk and talk some more, until a young forest officer agrees. They meet the women of&nbsp; the nearby villages.

“Okay,” she says, “Have it your way. But no excavators.” Geetu runs to her mother in worry. “But Maa, how can we cut a hill without a big machine?” “It’s okay, Geetu,” she says. “We are jal sahelis, the water is our friend. And friends help each other. We will find a way.” Word spreads: the jal sahelis are on a mission to bring the river home.

On the chosen day, the jal sahelis stand hand in hand near the hillock.&nbsp; They carry spades and axes and shovels. They look like a long ribbon twirled around it.

Together, they start digging. Day after day, more women join them.

They are building a waterway, they are inviting the river. And then it is done. The river gushes in and floods the pond. As the pond fills to the brim, the earth oozes water, too.

The jal sahelis don’t stop there. They clear other ponds of weeds. They build check dams to hold the rainwater. The water in the wells rises again. The next year’s rains fill the pond again. The hand pumps begin to sputter and gurgle and flow.

Maa and Kiran Chachi don’t have to walk miles to draw water. Maa walks in her chappals in front of the landlord, and no one dares say a word. The pebbles fall plip- plop- plip i nto the well.

The river is finally home. It gushes and gurgles and babbles—and goes splash when Geetu and Tipu jump in.

In Bundelkhand, a region spread across Madhya Pradesh and Uttar Pradesh, water is scarce. A&nbsp; changing climate is likely to make rainfall irregular and increase water insecurity. Sometimes, droughts last for years, drying up tanks, wells and rivers and making life difficult for women, whose work it is to fetch water. In 2011, during one such drought year, an organisation called Parmarth Samaj Sevi Sansthan started training the women of Bundelkhand to take ownership of the water resources of their villages. The jal sahelis worked with village officials to clean ponds, build small check dams, plant trees and revive rivers. Their work inspired others. They have grown into a group of more than 1,000 women in six districts of Uttar Pradesh and Madhya Pradesh.
