# The Stone Giants

One morning, Eilad and his Bei&nbsp; stroll through the lively Nartiang market enjoying themselves. Eilad, a sturdy boy with a friendly smile, eagerly helps his mother carry a munajun full of fresh groceries.

As they pass through the crowd, a Kong playfully calls out, “My, oh my, Deimaïa!&nbsp; This young lad is as strong as Mar Phalyngki himself!” Bei laughs as Eilad blushes, his chest puffing out with pride.

The ever-curious Eilad asks his mother, “Bei, who is Mar Phalyngki?” “Ah, Mar Phalyngki was the legendary man who brought those huge stones to our village.” “Those giant stones we pass by?” Eilad's eyes light up with&nbsp; excitement.&nbsp; “Will you tell me all about him?!”&nbsp;&nbsp; “Let’s head home first!” Bei chuckles.

Before they can leave the market, black clouds roll in as darkness descends upon Nartiang. Bei looks at Eilad and playfully remarks, “Aren’t you glad we got our knup for the day?” They put on their knup and start on their way back home.

Back home, Eilad eagerly says, "Bei, please tell me about Mar Phalyngki!&nbsp; Is he stronger than me? Can he carry more groceries?" Bei chuckles, "He's a powerhouse, Eilad. Think you can outdo him?" Eilad grins confidently, "Absolutely! I bet I can carry more than him!" Bei teases, "We'll see. But first, let me tell you his story."

“Mar Phalyngki’s mother Liti Bamon was all alone in the forest when he was born.” “All alone? How did they survive?” “It was tough. She wrapped him in the only piece of clothing she had on her, called Byrki . That’s why she changed his last name from Bamon to Phalyngki.”

She continues, “And you know what, that little boy grew up tossing around boulders like you toss pebbles! Imagine that!” Eilad laughs, picturing Mar Phalyngki&nbsp; as a boulder-tossing champion.

“Well, to relocate the goddess Iaw Blai from Raliang to our village, which created our Nartiang market,” Bei explains. “So, it was all thanks to the blessings of the goddess!” Eilad’s curiosity deepens. &nbsp;"But why did he carry those&nbsp; stones to Nartiang?"

Bei nods and continues, “The two made a deal in the end.” Intrigued, Eilad asks, “What kind of deal?” “In exchange for sacrifices, the goddess promised her protection for the community, particularly pregnant women.”

“So, is Iaw Blai still here?” “Absolutely! She protects the village, and helps your old&nbsp; Bei take care of young moms.” Eilad exclaims, “She’s our very own guardian angel!” Long after Bei finishes the story, it stays in Eilad's mind like a favorite movie.

Days later, with the sun beaming down on them, Eilad and his friends gather at the village playground. Eilad grins at his friends and suggests, "Let’s see who can make the best monolith!"

A neighborhood Ma passes by the playground. With a twinkle in his eye, he decides to tease them. &nbsp;"Hey, kids, what do you call a stone that never goes to school?" &nbsp;"I don't know, what?" "A skipping stone!"

“Ariii…That’s such a lame joke, Ma!”&nbsp; &nbsp;They laugh and start telling their own jokes as they continue to craft their monoliths.

&nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp;Glossary Bei: A Pna r word for mother Munajun: A traditional sling bag used by the people of the Jaiñtia community on market days Kong: Salutation for an older woman Knup: A traditonal umbrella worn by the people of the &nbsp;Jaiñtia community Ma: Salutation for an older man
