# Child's Play

Yet another skirmish ensued at the Sookhapur village well – this time between Sheela and Sunaina. But, honestly, who could blame them! The hand pump wasn’t working and both of them needed water. They both had families to feed, bathe and care for!

Villagers quarreling over their turn to draw water had become commonplace. Not just in this village, but around the world, poor villagers struggled to obtain water to sustain life. There had to be a solution to this.

Turns out, there was! This daily tussle moved Rohini, one of the only five girls allowed to study in the hitherto boys’ school at Sookhapur. Her mind conjured up a smorgasbord of solutions. They were advanced, yet required only rudimentary elements to construct and work.

Yet, nobody at Sookhapur could accept the fact that a little child, let alone a girl, could come up with ideas that could solve one of their primary problems. Every time she approached someone for help, they would walk away or change the topic.

While the others saw her as an annoying know-it-all, Rohini’s parents saw in her Sookhapur’s saviour. With their support, she managed to realise her best idea – a contraption involving Sookhapur’s famous merry-go-round.

The objective was to pump out groundwater to a tank which would cater to the entire village – the merry-go-round being the centre of this smart adaptation.

Connected to the merry-go-round was a generator upcycled from a discarded one, procured from the scrapyard that Rohini’s father was employed at.

She adapted the design from a book she had stumbled upon during one of her forages for knowledge at her school library.

Wow! Thanks to Rohini’s ingenuity, the cost-effective pump was up and running. Whenever children frolicked on the merry-go-round, the generated electricity powered a pump, drawing water to the tank.

The villagers were in absolute awe on witnessing this. Acknowledging the potential of education, they sent all their girls to school. At last, Rohini had earned the respect she always deserved and Sookhapur was no longer 'sookha'.
