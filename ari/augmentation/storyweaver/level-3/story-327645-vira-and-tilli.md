# Vira and Tilli

Vira woke up with butterflies in his stomach. Today, he was going with Aai and Baba to harvest onions in the field. He was thrilled to be meeting Tilli again.

A few months ago, Vira had helped sow onion saplings in the field. It was there that he first met Tilli. No one had paid much attention to Tilli. It hadn’t grown as well as the other saplings. But Vira had been hopeful.

He believed if he took care of it, Tilli would have the chance to grow into a healthy onion. If the sprinklers couldn’t reach Tilli, Vira poured the water himself.

He even ran behind flies to chase them out of the field.

After the tempo stopped, Vira ran into the onion field and searched for Tilli. He spotted the white pebble he had placed near it. Tilli stood gleaming in the sun. Some of its leaves had turned yellow and fallen down. Aai told him this was a good sign. “It shows that the onion bulbs are fully grown and ready to be harvested.”

Everyone started digging out the onions. After hours of effort, all the onions were unearthed. All except one. Aai nudged Vira towards Tilli. &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp;

Too many thoughts clouded Vira’s mind. What if Tilli hadn’t grown into a healthy bulb? Would Tilli leave him now? Vira froze and clutched onto his Aai. &nbsp; Aai held Vira by the hand and walked him over to Tilli. She started loosening the mud. Vira joined in and started digging. He pulled out the onion, and wiped off the mud covering it. There was a smile on his face. Tilli had turned into…

a round, shiny and purple bulb! Vira felt proud.

He held Tilli to his chest. He saw people holding sickles between their toes, separating the leaves from the bulbs. Vira cut Tilli’s leaves himself. They looked like tufts of hair on Tilli’s head.

Baba laid out large tarpaulin sheets. The onions were spread out on them to dry for five days.

Vira held Tilli tighter to his chest. “Vira, do you want to take Tilli home for the five days?” Baba asked. Vira said a loud &nbsp;“ Yes! ”

At dinner, Aai and Baba spoke about how good the yield was that year. “We may have to take a bigger tempo to carry the onions to the mandi,” said Aai. Vira felt uneasy about Tilli going away. He ate very little.

For the next few days, Vira kept returning to the room to look at Tilli. He couldn’t stop admiring it.

“Baba, Aai can I keep Tilli with me? Can you please not sell Tilli at the mandi?” Tears rolled down Vira’s eyes. “Vira, how long can you keep it with you? Tilli will start to go bad in a few weeks,” said Baba.

Vira said, “Then it’ll be better to sell Tilli with the other onions.”&nbsp; “Good decision, Vira. This way, Tilli will become delicious food for someone,” said Aai. Vira imagined Tilli being picked up by a famous chef who would invent a new dish with it. He smiled.

Next morning, the onions were filled into gunny sacks. These sacks were loaded onto the tempo. Vira sat snug between the sacks with Tilli in his hands.

At the gate of the mandi, Baba called out, “120 quintal, Nashik red onion.” The person at the gate entered these details into a computer and handed a slip to Baba.

Baba laid out some onions for display. Traders came to check their quality. The onions were sold to the trader who bid the highest price. Baba looked happy. Each sack was weighed and loaded onto the trader’s truck.

Baba turned towards Vira. He smiled and placed Tilli in the last sack boarding the truck.

Grow Your Own Tilli - Cut the bottom half of an onion. - Leave it out to dry for a day.&nbsp; - Hang the onion cutting over a small bowl of water with its root bottom just touching the water. It will grow white roots in a few days.&nbsp; - Plant the onion cutting in a pot of soil. Place the pot in a sunny spot and water it every day.&nbsp; - Give a name to your onion and wait for it to grow! Did You Know? - Nashik red onion is a variety of onion grown in Nashik, Maharashtra. - A mandi is a wholesale market where produce is sold in bulk quantities. Nashik’s Lasalgaon mandi is the largest wholesale onion market in India. - Traders purchase the produce in bulk quantities at the mandi and sell it in small amounts to shopkeepers, who sell it to people like you.
