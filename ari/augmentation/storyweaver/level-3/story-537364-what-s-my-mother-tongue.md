# What's My Mother Tongue?

“Iza, tomorrow is Language Day at school.&nbsp; Ms Kivini has asked us to introduce ourselves in our mother tongue,” says Zhevili.&nbsp; “She said our mother tongue is the language we speak at home.”

“That will be fun! You will hear so many languages,” says Iza. “Yes, Lanuakum is Ao, Chuimiwon is Tangkhul, Loreni is Lotha. They are all different Naga people,” says Ipu.

“Oh! Ipu, does that mean I am the only English person in my class?” asks Zhevili.&nbsp; “No, Zhevili darling. You are not English,” says Ipu.

“But I speak English at home,” says Zhevili. “Yes, you do. But you are Sümi Naga and your mother tongue is Sümi,” says Ipu.

“Ipu, we also need to dress up when we introduce ourselves tomorrow,” says Zhevili.&nbsp; “Okay... let’s find something beautiful for you to wear tomorrow," says Iza. "Would you like to wear this amini, these bangles… ?”&nbsp;

“Yes! I love all the jewellery. But what shall I say?” asks Zhevili. “You will say, ‘I je ye Zhevili’ in Sümi,” Iza tells her. “I je ye Zhevili, I je ye Zhevili, I je ye Zhevili.”

Next day at school...

Lanuakum says in Ao,&nbsp; “Kü tenung Lanuakum.” Chuimiwon says in Tangkhul, “Iwui aming Chuimiwon hoyei.” Loreni says in Lotha,&nbsp; “Aa ming jo Loreni.”

Fangkei says in Konyak,&nbsp; “Kote vei Fangkei ja tong.” Riyanla says in Yimkhiung,&nbsp; “I mung ju Riyanla ah.” And Zhevili says in Sümi,&nbsp; &nbsp;“I je ye Zhevili.”

There are over 50 different Naga languages and dialects of these languages in Nagaland and its neighbouring regions where Naga people live. The UNESCO has listed many Naga languages and dialects as “vulnerable” among the endangered languages.&nbsp; That means most children speak these languages, but only at home or in their social circles. Konyak, Tangkhul, Yimkhiung, Phom, Angami, Lotha, Sümi, Ao, Rengma, Sangtam, Khiamniungan, Pochury and Mao are some of those languages. Source: http://www.unesco.org/languages-atlas
