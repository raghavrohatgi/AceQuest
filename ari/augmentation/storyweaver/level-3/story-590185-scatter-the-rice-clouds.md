# Scatter the Rice Clouds

“Look Bor,” Iba pointed at the deep blue sky covered with fluffy white clouds.&nbsp; &nbsp;“It looks like blobs of rice pudding!” Little Bor laughed at his sister.&nbsp; “It does, Kong kong! They look like ja-shulia , my favourite sticky rice!”

Iba and Bor grew up among lush forests and paddy fields. Their village was famous for rice. They often saw elders making delicious putharo rice cakes and rice drinks with jarut .

A soft meowing sound appeared from behind them. Kemnai, their cat, had crept in.&nbsp; “Kumno, Kemnai!” Iba said, picking up the fluffy creature. “What do you think of the clouds?” “Purrrrr,” Kemnai answered, running out of Iba’s arms. &nbsp;Off they went after Kemnai, following his lead.

Swoosh!&nbsp; They ran past the elders and the rice field. Suddenly, the sky began to change.&nbsp; It grew darker and thicker, changing from rice cakes to grey dust, casting a shadow over the village. Iba and Bor grew worried.

“What is happening, Kong kong?” Bor asked, looking up at the sky. “I’m not sure,” Iba said, watching the clouds intently.&nbsp; “But it looks like a storm is coming.”

&nbsp;As soon as she said those words, a thunderous sound echoed through the fields. Pitter-patter, large raindrops had begun to fall. Iba used her apron to keep them dry. “Bor! Kemnai has led us close to the shed where the elders work. Let us go there,” Iba yelled, pointing at the shed in sight. They quickly ran inside to escape the rain.

Lo and behold! Mama, their uncle, was there. “Oh, children!” Mama said,&nbsp; “I was just taking the rice inside. Come, come, help me before it gets soggy from the rain.” Iba and Bor sprang to help.

“Yes, Mama. Come Bor, we must save the rice from getting spoiled,” said Iba. They were able to take the rice drying on the shylliah inside.

“Thank you, children,” Mama said while he lit a lamp, shining a warm glow on their faces. “Looks like we’ll be waiting here till the rain stops,”&nbsp; he said as they looked out of the window.

The children noticed the items that were lying around Mama’s shed. “Mama, can you show us what you are working on,” they asked.

Thud…. ﻿ Thud…. Thud…. “Of course. We are going to grind the rice to make a fine powder,” Mama said, taking out his synrei in hand and thlong , starting to pound the rice. Thud-thud-thud, he pounded the rice. The children marvelled at Mama’s strength and the way the grains danced in the thlong .

Thump! A loud sound filled the room.&nbsp; Kemnai had gotten onto the pdung and scattered the rice everywhere.&nbsp; “Oh no! Catch him children! I hope the rice is not spoilt!” Mama shouted with alarm. The children grabbed the little feline by its fur. &nbsp;“Meowww,” he resisted. “He’s made a mess,” groaned Iba.&nbsp; But Bor noticed something else.

“He’s made rice clouds!” he gleefully pointed out.&nbsp; “See Mama?” Looking down at the scattered rice, indeed they saw specs of grain merged into clouded clusters. “You’re right Bor!” Mama’s concern melted into a smile,&nbsp; struck by his nephew’s imagination.&nbsp; “You’ll see many kinds in the sky.&nbsp; But only these will be made by a cat’s paws,” he chuckled.

Just then, the clouds began to scatter and break apart, just like the rice. Soon, the clouds cleared up, painting the great big sky with the colours of a rainbow.

"Are you sure Kemnai isn’t a magical cat?” smiled Mama looking at the clear open sky. Iba and Bor laughed and hugged Kemnai, grateful for his playful spirit that had scattered the clouds and brought a joyful sky to their village.

From that day on, whenever the sky was filled with clouds,&nbsp; Iba and Bor would look&nbsp; for Kemnai and rice to play with, hoping to scatter the clouds once again&nbsp; and create more rainbows for their village to enjoy.

Spot the Hidden Animals! There are many types of clouds. The clouds Kemnai made are called ‘Altocumulous’&nbsp; clouds because of their shape in the sky. Can you spot the animal shapes in the altocumulous clouds?&nbsp; Try to find all of them!

Glossary Putharo: A common Khasi snack made of pulverised good quality rice by a steaming method. Jarut: Rice to be mixed with yeast for the purpose of distilling liquor. Kumno: Used colloquially as a greeting meaning ‘how are you’. Kong kong: An elder sister or any grown up girl is addressed as Kong kong. Shylliah: A mat woven out of bamboo splits. Synrei:&nbsp; A pestle generally made of wood. Thlong:&nbsp; A wooden mortar for husking paddy. Pdung: A circular tray, made out of bamboo splits, used for drying seeds, farm produce, etc. This story is based on ‘Ki Ktien-Tymmen’ or 'Words of the Elders': ‘Ki Ktien-Tymmen’ are a group of sayings/proverbs that are used in the Khasi language to&nbsp; describe natural phenomena or teach lessons. ‘Phriang jarut ka Miaw’: This particular saying, where ‘phriang jarut’ means scattering rice and ‘ka Miaw’ means a cat, compares the playfulness of a cat scattering lumps of rice used in making local fermented&nbsp;rice beer to the phenomena and formation of altocumulous clouds.
