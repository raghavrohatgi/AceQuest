# Lungdai and the Hornbill

It had many tall trees with hollow trunks and branches filled with golden mangoes and ripe papayas. The rustling leaves and a shallow gentle stream made it a nice place to relax on a hot summer day. *Pipi: a Hrangkhawl word for grandmother. Pipi’s* garden was Lungdai’s favourite place in the whole world.

Lungdai ran towards the papaya trees, a skip in his steps. “So many juicy papayas! I will…. gasp Pipi, look!” Lungdai cried. “That bird is eating my papaya! Oh no, they are taking all the fruit! Shoo! Shoo! Go away, bad bird!” “Do you think the papayas are fully ripe, Pipi?” “Yes, they are,” Pipi said, smiling.&nbsp;&nbsp; “How would you like to eat them? Small pieces or cut lengthwise?” “I want them sliced lengthwise,” Lungdai replied. “Alright! As you wish.” “Yay, I can’t wait!” “Ah, let it be. It’s a hornbill bird,” Pipi explained. “But it’s taking and destroying all the fruits. Chase it away! Shoo!”

“Now, now, let it be,” Pipi said, gently pulling him back. “We have plenty, so let them have some.&nbsp; Also, look up there.”&nbsp; “What? Not just the papaya, but all the fruits?” Lungdai was annoyed. “They will finish everything.&nbsp; This is your tree Pipi,&nbsp; why don’t they go somewhere else?” Lungdai followed Pipi’s hand as she pointed to the hornbill bird which landed on the mango tree. “That’s a dada hornbill going to feed his family.&nbsp; It uses the fruit to feed the mama and the baby inside their nest,” Pipi said. “Oh? But I didn’t plant this tree,” Pipi replied, “who knows, maybe this hornbill did it.” Lungdai was shocked. “The bird did it?”

“Hornbills do more than eat fruit, my dear. They help the forest grow. When they eat, they scatter seeds, planting new trees. Without them, many trees would not be there in the forest, and certainly not in this garden” Pipi explained. Pipi let out a sigh, “While the forest is their home, they are disappearing. You must have learned this in school, right?” “So, the forest is their home?” Lungdai asked, a lot less annoyed now. Lungdai recalled how his teacher had told them about trees being cut down, the river drying up and the smoke filling the air.

“When their homes disappear, they have to look for a new place to live, which is very difficult,” Pipi continued. “And sometimes, there are some wicked people who even kill them. Now tell me, dear, should we not protect them?” Lungdai thought about the hornbill bird flying over the dried-up river, choking on the smoky air. Will the air clear? Will the river flow again? Will we find a home? “How sad,” Lungdai quietly whispered.

“Hornbills do more than eat fruit, my dear. They help the forest grow. When they eat, they scatter seeds, planting new trees. Without them, many trees would not be there in the forest, and certainly not in this garden” Pipi explained. “So, the forest is their home?” Lungdai asked, a lot less annoyed now. Lungdai quietly watched the hornbill gently pick up the ripe fruit and fly near the hollow bark of the tree. It brought the fruit close to the hole in the trunk, and another beak stretched out, taking the fruit. Lungdai’s face lit up, and he said, “Pipi! Pipi! Did you see that? Dada hornbill just put the fruit inside the hole!” “I did. That's the mama bird, who stays inside with the baby bird. They wait for the food that the dada brings them,” Pipi explained.

“Pipi, what if there are no fruits?” Lungdai watched the hornbill family, the dada passing the fruit through the small hole. “Well, the dada bird flies and flies till it can bring back food. After all, the mama and the baby are waiting for it. The mama protects the baby inside the nest while the dada brings them food. That’s how the baby stays safe, Lungdai,” Pipi said. “Sometimes only fruit is not enough. Mama hornbill needs nutritious insects, which they don’t usually get nowadays. Therefore, they have to fly far away to get food,” Pipi added. “What if…what if the dada doesn’t come back?” Lungdai asked. Pipi sighed. “Then the poor birds would have nothing to eat, and they would die.” Lungdai couldn’t hold back tears at the thought of that happening.

“It’s alright, my dear.” Pipi gently patted his shoulder. “These birds are safe.” “I am sorry I chased you away earlier. Here, you can feed your family.” He laid the papaya on the ground and slowly backed away. “I understand now, Pipi,” Lungdai said, “this papaya is not just mine, but it is for all of us to share.” He turned to the hornbill bird sitting up at the branch. Then, he made his way to the papaya tree and grabbed a juicy papaya. A few minutes later, the bird swooped in and took a piece of the fruit.

“Pipi, look, it took it!” Lungdai cried. “That’s the spirit, my child,” Pipi said with a chuckle. “You have learnt how to share. Now come along; let me cut up some of the papaya for you as well.” Pipi’s garden was still Lungdai’s favourite place in the world. However, now, along with the tall trees and juicy fruits, he also looked forward to meeting the little bird family that had made this garden their home.

About the story Human-Hornbill Relations in Tripura In Tripura, the oriental pied hornbill (Anthracoceros albirostris) shares a long-standing relationship with the state’s indigenous communities, such as the Molsom and Hrangkhawl communities. This is echoed in their beliefs, folktales, oral traditions, and everyday practices that are aimed at protecting these birds. These communities consider the hornbill bird sacred and strictly forbid their killing or for any of their body parts to be used for any decoration (like as part of a costume).

One of the key reasons for the traditional prohibition against hunting hornbills is rooted in the belief that anyone who kills a hornbill will be cursed, leading to the death of a family member in a future generation. While this may be seen as superstition, this very belief system shows an understanding of the bird’s monogamous nature and their vital role in the ecosystem and hence is a way to protect the hornbills. Especially during the nesting period, the female hornbill remains sealed inside the nest until the eggs hatch and the chicks are ready to fly. During this time, which lasts around 6-8 months, the male hornbill is solely responsible for feeding the female hornbill. Killing the male hornbill is thus seen as the killing of an entire family. This belief system reinforces the perspective that protecting hornbills is not just an act of biodiversity conservation but also an expression of traditional ecological knowledge and a deep-rooted sense of coexistence between humans and nature/animals. Beyond the hornbill’s cultural and spiritual significance, these birds play a vital role in forest regeneration by dispersing seeds and contributing to biodiversity – an aspect that is well understood by the indigenous (Molsom and Hrangkhawl) communities of Tripura.
