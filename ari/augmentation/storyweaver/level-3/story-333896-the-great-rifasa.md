# The Great Rifasa

Nothing seemed right. By now, the clouds should have cooled the land with many showers. But the water holes were still dry. All the animals of the Kabini forest were hot and thirsty.

One day, things got stranger still. The sun was already peeking past the highest trees. But something was missing. Where had the human tourists in their safari jeeps gone? They never missed a day, come rain or shine. They should have been driving around noisily everywhere, disturbing everything.

The animals began to get very curious. Surely, the humans must be somewhere! The sloth bears, with their sharp snouts, started sniffing around. “Here, Papa, here,” shouted a bear cub. “Diesel smell, diesel smell!” Papa bear shuffled to his son and inhaled deeply. “Good catch, Cubby, but those are not fresh. Keep trying.”

All over Kabini, the animals began their own safari — The Great Rifasa. It was to hunt down the lost human tourists and their jungle jeeps. They decided to search all the favourite tourist spots.

Some elephants and chital deer strolled to the backwaters of the Kabini dam. They must be here somewhere, thought the animals. But there were no jeeps to be found.

Kajal, the beautiful tigress, took her two young sons to the temple tank. “No doubt they will be looking for us here,” she thought. But after a long wait, she too had to give up. No humans today.

“They will be looking for us at&nbsp; S-Bend,”&nbsp; growled Chukki, the tigress. “Let’s go&nbsp; there.” She beckoned&nbsp; her&nbsp; three young&nbsp; cubs and&nbsp; their older brother. But the entire stretch till the&nbsp; Power&nbsp; Line was very quiet.

Round and round the Kabini forest, all the animals went. They searched along the water bodies. They trekked up to the highest point in the forest and down into the deepest valley. The langurs and the bonnet macaques peered from the tallest trees. The serpent eagles and the hawk eagles soared above the clouds, looking for the moving vehicles. But they spotted no jeeps, no humans. There was no click, click, click of cameras. No binoculars glinting in the sun. Nothing. The animals of the Kabini forest could not rest that night. The next day, and the day after that, it was the same story all over again. The humans had simply disappeared.

For a while, some of the animals enjoyed the peace. Good riddance, they thought. Now they could freely wander about and lie on the roads. The predators could hunt in the open. Everyone could breathe cleaner air. But soon, animal after animal started to tell a different story.

“My cubs love to run away and play. Sometimes, the humans find them even before I can,” confessed Naama, the tigress. “Let’s hope they come back soon.”

The elephant mother trumpeted in agreement. “They can be irritating,” she said, “but they build paths for us to cross the jungle and avoid the prickly lantana plants.” “Yes, and I do pretend to charge at them sometimes,” Tambu, the young tusker, laughed. “It is so boring without them.” The Kabini animals decided to act. After many years, the Jungle Council was called. Mighty old Bhogeshwara, the long-tusked elephant, took charge. He commanded the langurs to gather all the animals. Soon the jungle was abuzz with their myriad coughs and screams. “Come for an important meeting,” they called out. “You will all be safe. No predator will attack. Come, come to the backwaters now.”

One by one, all the animals came out to the open grassland near the water. Elephants and big cats, deer and monkeys, squirrels and jungle fowl, bears and gaurs, otters and crocodiles. What a spectacular sight it was.

“My friends and, well, a few enemies,” harrumphed Bhogeshwara.&nbsp; “We are gathered&nbsp; here today because of a tragedy that might affect us all. The humans are suffering from a great illness. My cousins from the nearby zoo told me about it. The disease they call COVID-19 is so dangerous that they are hiding in their homes. Earlier they were like us,” he looked at the elephants around him. “They used to live in herds. Now they have to be like you, Chukki,” he said to the mother tigress. “They have to stay just with their own families. Maybe they are not visiting so as to keep us safe. Isn’t that kind? But they may not return anytime soon.”

“Does that mean we can roam freely now?” Scarface, the leopard, sawed. “A jeep nearly ran over me the other day,” he said, shuddering at the memory. Doey, the fawn, stepped forward. “But I like humans,” she said, boldly. “They always smile and wave at me. No tiger attacks us when they are around.” “I too love the tourists,” Paara, the peacock, preened. “They truly appreciate my beauty. Do you know how many photos they click of me dancing?”

The langurs only laughed. “Oh, we can easily control them,” said Langa, the biggest of them all. “As soon as we call, they gather around us, pointing their big cameras here and there. I often give false alarms just to fool them. It is fun.” “Let’s face it. We miss them,” declared Spanner, the tiger. Many animals nodded.

“You? You miss them?” came a sneering voice&nbsp; from the&nbsp; bushes. Karia, the world-famous black panther, emerged&nbsp; like a silky silhouette against the sky. “Excuse me! It is&nbsp; I who should miss them the most. For six years, people&nbsp; from all over the world have come here for me and only&nbsp; me! I just have to yawn and cameras click like gunfire!&nbsp; All of you,” he looked around disdainfully,&nbsp; “are only the&nbsp; surplus of the safari. After all, I am&nbsp; the only black&nbsp; panther humans can hope to spot.”

The other animals looked at Karia, who had jumped onto a fallen log. He was rather handsome, his yellow eyes glistening, his tail curling. But why so much arrogance? Some of the tigers were amused. The leopards were not. “But when will they return? Don’t they miss me? “ Karia continued, quite unaware of Scarface’s baleful look. Only a loud silence echoed through the forest.

“All right then, we agree that the tourist humans mean us no harm,” trumpeted Bhogeshwara. “Come, let us pray for them to be safe enough to return. We will continue our Rifasa until then.” The Kabini animals bowed their heads. From the mighty elephants to the little mongoose babies, everyone prayed for the well-being of the humans. The forest became still.

Maybe the animals of Kabini crafted some magic that day. In cities and towns, the humans too were missing the forests. While praying for the health of their loved ones, they saved some wishes for the wild animals they tried to protect.

At last, scientists developed vaccines against the virus! When the pandemic receded and the lockdown was lifted, nature lovers rushed back to Kabini. A few animals were on a Rifasa the morning they heard the first rumble of the jeeps. “Hurrah!” the elephant herd trumpeted, letting the whole jungle know the humans had returned.

What a treat awaited the tourists that day! The tigers, the bears — even the elusive Karia —&nbsp; came right up to the jeeps, almost as though to say, “Welcome back. We know you love us. We missed you too.” As the human safari started again, the animals finally ended their Great Rifasa.
