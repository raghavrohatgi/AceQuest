# Ilari's Jaiñsem

Ilari lived with her family in Roibha village.&nbsp; Lari, as she was lovingly called, was a curious little girl, fond of learning new things.

Mei&nbsp;was getting ready for Ka Shad Suk Mynsiem. “Lari, I will be back late tonight. Have a nice time with&nbsp;Mei ieit&nbsp;while I am gone,” Mei said as she gave Lari a kiss and left. Wow! Mei looks beautiful!

“Mei ieit, could you teach me how to dress like Mei?” &nbsp;Lari asked her wise grandmother.&nbsp; “I too want to look like the ladies at the festival!” “Of course, my dear,” said Mei ieit with a gentle smile.&nbsp; “Jaiñsems are important in our culture.&nbsp; Let me show you how to wear it.”

Mei ieit shows Lari a drawer full of fabrics.&nbsp; “Wow!” Lari exclaims. “Before wearing a jaiñsem, we wear a blouse and skirt first.&nbsp; Let us find you something like that,” said Mei ieit. “I will get them!” Lari scrambles to get the clothes. “And done!” She emerges triumphantly from the heap. Mei ieit laughs, amused and impressed. “A jaiñsem is very special. Wear it always with pride and joy,” she says, picking out a nylon jaiñsem for Lari.

Lari looked confused.&nbsp; “Mei ieit, this one is different from Mei’s jaiñsem, isn’t it?” she said, pointing at the patterned fabric. “You are right dear. Mei had a silk jaiñsem called a ' dhara ', which is worn on special festival days. This one I’ve picked out is one you can wear everyday,” said Mei ieit. Silk Dhara Kyrwoh Shrong Nylon Jaiñsem

“Now, a jaiñsem is made of two similar rectangular pieces.” Mei ieit takes the first piece, letting the fabric fall lightly. “Wow, Mei ieit. The colours remind me of spring!” Lari exclaims.

“Remember, this side with the colours is the front side. Don’t make a mistake. You wouldn’t want to wear a shirt backwards now, would you? It is the same with a jaiñsem.” “That would be funny,” &nbsp;Lari giggles.

Mei ieit then began folding the top to make sure the length just rests on Lari’s tiny ankles. Lari looks at the beautifully designed border, “I see the shrong you mentioned Mei ieit.” “Good eye, Lari. Remember, that always goes on the bottom,” she said, pointing to the delicate thread work on the edge of the fabric.

“Let me show you how it is done.&nbsp; Wrap the fabric over your body, under your arm and tie or pin the corners together over your right shoulder,” Mei ieit says, while Lari watches eagerly. “Go on, now try it on your own,” she urged, “and be careful about the length.”

“Splendid my dear!” Mei ieit chuckles and applauds. Lari marvels at her handiwork but quickly realises something.&nbsp; “Why does it have to be pinned to the right shoulder first?” she asks. Seeing her granddaughter's keen observation, Mei ieit paused to tell Lari one more thing she needed to learn.

“It is tradition that we never tie it on the left side first,” said Mei ieit.&nbsp; “It is our way of showing the difference between life and death&nbsp; and is only done at the very end of one’s life.” “I never knew that Mei ieit.&nbsp;&nbsp; Now that I know, I will wear it correctly,”&nbsp; said Lari confidently.

Mei ieit then draped a checked shawl over Lari to complete the look. “Wow, I look just like you!” exclaimed Lari.&nbsp; “Yes, you do,” said Mei ieit&nbsp;with a warm embrace. Right at that moment, Lari’s mother returned from the festival.

“Mei! Mei! Look what Mei ieit taught me!” said Lari,&nbsp; twirling in her&nbsp; new attire. “Ani! You look beautiful, my girl,” her mother replied,&nbsp; surprised&nbsp; and delighted to find Lari looking so happy and vibrant.

Lari was thrilled that she discovered something new that day. “Now I can wear my new jaiñsem to the next festival!”

Glos sary Mei: A Khasi word for mother. Mei ieit: A Khasi word for maternal grandmother. Ka Shad Suk Mynsiem (The Dance of Peaceful Hearts): A traditional annual spring dance festival performed to celebrate &nbsp;harvesting and sowing in the month of April. Dhara: A special silk version of the jaiñsem worn on special occasions. The silk varieties include Muga and Eri silk with a tasselled border. Shrong: Embroidered border design. It can vary from a simple thin design to a thicker border for a more elaborate look.

Step 1:&nbsp; A blouse and skirt are worn first. Step 2:&nbsp; First rectangular piece goes over the body with the ‘shrong’ at the bottom. Step 3:&nbsp; The two corners are tied or pinned on the right shoulder. Step 4:&nbsp; Next, the second piece is draped in a similar manner with the corners tied or pinned on the left shoulder. Step 5:&nbsp; The lengths of both pieces can be checked to make sure they are the same. The look is complete and may be worn with other accessories like jewellery or shawls, as per the desire of the wearer. Common Mistakes Mistake 1: Amateurs&nbsp; may sometimes wear the cloth in a hurry or wear it without paying attention, which may result in the hem stitching side being shown in front. This is often funny but usually can go undetected if the material itself is such that the front and back look the same. Mistake 2: Amateurs may drape the left side first over the right side. This is incorrect as this is only done during the funeral of a Khasi woman.&nbsp; Instructions on How to Wear a Jaiñsem
