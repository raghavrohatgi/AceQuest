# Asha's Voice

Asha is very, very small.

Asha is too small to flip the roti on the hot, hot tava. Whenever she tries, she burns her fingers. So Asha hums a cooking song while the dough puffs into warm, fluffy pillows.

Asha is too small to call Farida Aunty or any of her other neighbours. She can’t read their names in Mummy’s phone. So Asha hums a calling song while Mummy makes calls.

Mummy calls everyone to remind them about today’s meeting with Jothi Madam, the village’s Member of Parliament. She reminds them to bring their ideas about how to help children in the village grow healthy and strong.

Asha is too small to buy anything from Kareem Uncle’s shop. She doesn’t know how to count out change yet. So Asha hums a shopping song while Kuni Didi pays for a brand-new pen.

Asha is too small to do most things. But she is big enough to do some things.

Asha is big enough to pass out juice and rotis to her neighbours who come to the meeting. Farida Aunty and Kareem Uncle ask for seconds, and then thirds. Asha hums a full-tummy song.

Asha is big enough to find the coolest spot to sit beneath the banyan tree. She leads her neighbours there. Asha hums a sweet-happy song.

Asha is big enough to tell Kuni Didi the names of their neighbours to record in the register. Her sister’s new pen makes a scratch-scratch rhythm. Asha hums a ready-to-go song.

Under the banyan tree, Asha hums a waiting song. Asha doesn’t mind waiting. But her neighbours are big people with big responsibilities. “I have to do the cooking,” Jamuna Aunty says. “I have to get water from the pump,” Farida Aunty says. “I have to re-open my store,” Kareem Uncle says.

Mummy phones the MP. But Jothi Madam is still on her way. The villagers start to murmur. Their voices are a soft growl. “So late?” they say. “We’ve been here all afternoon.”

Their murmuring grows and grows, louder and louder. It becomes a roar. “We can’t stay here forever,” Kareem Uncle says. He gets up to leave. Mummy and Daddy and Kuni Didi look nervous. But Asha isn’t nervous.

Because Asha is big enough to know what to do. Asha remembers a song her Aunty taught her. It’s a coming-together song. It’s a making-change song. It’s the perfect song to make the time go by. Asha stands up tall. She begins to sing.

Hum honge kamyab, hum honge kamyab, hum honge kamyab, ek din… Ho, mann mein hai vishvas, pura hai vishvas, hum honge kamyab ek din. We shall overcome, we shall overcome, we shall overcome, some day... Oh, deep in my heart, I do believe, we shall overcome some day.

First, Jamuna Aunty sings along. Next, Kareem Uncle. Then Farida Aunty, and Mummy and Daddy and even Kuni Didi, who says she doesn’t like to sing. Before long, everyone is singing. And no one is asking about the time.

Suddenly, the villagers hear a rumble-rumble and a sputter-sputter-sputter. It is Jothi Madam! She pulls up on her scooter. When she hears the song, she begins to sing too. The MP and the villagers finish the verse. Everybody cheers.

“What a wonderful way to start a meeting,” Jothi Madam says. “Whose idea was this?” “Mine,” Asha says. “My name is Asha.” “Do you have any other ideas, Asha?” the MP asks. “Yes,” Asha says. “Lots!”

Asha tells Jothi Madam her ideas: Planting a community garden for the anganwadi.

Creating a lending library in the community hall. Building a clinic that gives free check-ups. And starting music lessons for all the children in the primary school. Asha talks and talks, and Jothi Madam listens and smiles.

Asha may be small… but her voice is very, very big.
