# We Hope: Children on Climate Change

Authors Abhivakti Mishra, Shabnam Biswas, Sathvik, Yash Rajendra Dole, Dilip Mashya, Pooja Bhadoria, Ronak Kumar, Atharva Raut, Lakshika Soni, Mehran Shafi, Bethany, Poorna Nama, Mahesh Nayak, Abhiraj, Ayush Jadhav Illustrators Canato Jimo, Prabha Mallya, David Yambem, Arati Kumar-Rao, The Big Fat Bao, Archana Sreenivasan, Rohan Dahotre, Karunya Baskar, Sudarshan Shaw, Kanak Shashi, Ogin Nayam, Priya Kuriyan, Studio Babakiki Additional text&nbsp; Radha Rangarajan

Jellyfish glow in the sunlit sea. Bulbuls sing songs. Foxes wrap their tails like a blanket. What a wonderful world we live in! But, things have changed. Today, we live in a climate-changed world, one altered by us humans. Some of these changes we can observe and feel, some we cannot. Climate change means different things to different people. The rain that flooded homes. The snow that came down as an avalanche. The forest that breathed fire like a dragon. The warm ocean that ate up the beach. The too-hot sun that scorched their crops. Illustration by Canato Jimo

These signals are telling us that something is very wrong. That things need to be fixed right away, while we still have time to fix them. Together, we can restore our planet, and create a better world for all of us. Illustration by Canato Jimo

Climate change is the alarming result&nbsp; of human greed. Abhivakti Mishra , 16, Navy Children School,&nbsp; Port&nbsp; Blair, Andaman and Nicobar Islands (Dakshin&nbsp; Foundation) With rising temperatures, we are seeing&nbsp; sea-level rise. Our life on our island will&nbsp; be difficult. Shabnam Biswas , 13, Bharatiya Vidya Bhavan Mondal Public School, Madhupur, Diglipur, North Andaman Island (Dakshin Foundation) Illustration by Prabha Mallya

When the Earth gets warmer, oceans take in the heat, causing water levels to rise. As it is hot, glaciers melt, increasing the amount of water&nbsp; in the oceans. When waters heat up, coral reefs lose all colour and&nbsp; life. The wildlife and people of Pacific islands such as Tuvalu, Kiribati&nbsp; and Nauru are among the first victims of climate change. India’s own&nbsp; Andaman &amp; Nicobar Islands might not be inhabitable by 2100. Over 40&nbsp; island nations around the world have come together to draw attention&nbsp; to the fact that they will end up being climate refugees when their&nbsp; island homes disappear.

There is a lake near my house which has&nbsp;water only during the monsoon. The rest&nbsp;of the time it is dry due to the heat. Sathvik , 15, SVS High School, Agumbe, Karnataka&nbsp; (Kalinga Centre for Rainforest Ecology) Illustration by David Yambem

The village of Agumbe gets the most rainfall in south India. But with&nbsp;climate change, the rainfall has become irregular. If the monsoons are late or early, there’s somebody who will tell&nbsp;you. The beautiful Malabar gliding frogs come out of hiding during the&nbsp;monsoon to breed. They lay their eggs in a foamy nest that hangs over&nbsp;a stream. That way, the tadpoles can land safely in water when they are&nbsp;born. Pay close attention to the frogs’ croaks, and you will know if the&nbsp;stream is too dry or too full. Imagine a world where their tadpoles are&nbsp;never born, a world without their song.

There is no water in our village taps. I have to walk long distances with my mummy to fetch water. Yash Rajendra Dole , 13, Dhanora, Maharashtra&nbsp; (Abhiyan-Nalanda Library) Illustration by Arati Kumar-Rao

Groundwater levels go down because of over-extraction. People pump out water faster than the rain can replenish it. This situation gets worse when there’s a drought or the rainfall patterns change. When drinking water is scarce, women and girls are affected the most. They end up having to walk over 20km to fetch water for the family. Sometimes they make this trip every single day. Many times a day. This is why communities are coming together to be better prepared for drought, by managing water resources. They are recharging groundwater, growing indigenous crops like jowar and bajra instead of cotton and sugarcane, and saving rainwater for the harsh summers.

Our village used to look like a forest.&nbsp; Now it does not look like a forest. Dilip Mashya , 13, Tamarind Tree School,&nbsp; Dahanu, Maharashtra Illustration by The Big Fat Bao

Did you know that trees can cool down a city’s temperature by upto 12°C? Yet, forests are being felled in Dahanu to make way for industries, contributing to global warming. This is bad news for a special visitor to this coastal town. The Olive Ridley sea turtle comes to Dahanu’s shore to lay eggs every year. When an egg is laid, no one knows whether the baby will be male or female. The turtle doesn’t know. Not even the egg knows. If the weather stays cool, a male is born. If it stays warm, a female is born! With global warming, more female turtles will be born than males, which affects the fragile balance of their entire population.

The turtles are dying. Pooja Bhadoria , 15, Mahatma Gandhi Sainik Inter&nbsp; Coll, Panchai Gaon, Etawah, Uttar Pradesh&nbsp; (Gharial Ecology Project) Illustration by Archana Sreenivasan

The red-crowned roof turtle basks in the sun on the sandbanks of the Chambal River. It is so rare that less than 500 adult turtles survive in the world. Why? They’ve been hunted down or have gotten caught in fishing nets. The sand in which they lay eggs has been taken away from rivers, along with the very water that gives them life. Climate change has made temperatures rise and dried up rivers, which affects many other animals as well. Such as the gharial, a type of freshwater crocodile whose population has gone down by over 98 per cent since 1940. Across Chambal, people are working hard to protect the turtle and gharial from extinction, sometimes one egg at a time.

Climate change snatches homes&nbsp; and&nbsp; habitats away from humans&nbsp; and&nbsp; animals alike. Ronak Kumar , 15, Sikanderpur, Haryana (The Community Library Project) Illustration by Rohan Dahotre

For centuries, Haryana’s grasslands were home to an antelope&nbsp; called the blackbuck. Grasslands can be as rich as forests, not just&nbsp; with wildlife. They also absorb a lot of carbon from the atmosphere.&nbsp; But grasslands are often treated as wastelands. They are taken over&nbsp; illegally, and farms, homes and factories are built over them. Roads&nbsp; and railway lines pierce through them. With increasing heat and drought, both grasslands and blackbucks are in danger. Saving one&nbsp; means saving the other.

My lungs are tired. Atharva Raut , 15, Akshara High&nbsp; School,&nbsp; Mumbai, Maharashtra Illustration by Karunya Baskar

Mumbai is one of the most polluted cities in the world. Emissions from industries and traffic, burning garbage, construction dust, and untreated sewage make it worse. Thankfully, mangrove forests, Mumbai’s natural coast guards, absorb eight times more carbon dioxide than any other ecosystem! They are our shields in the battle against climate change, preventing flooding and soil erosion. They are the first line of defence during cyclonic storms and tsunamis, extreme weather patterns that are increasing due to climate change.

I feel insecure about my future in a climate-changed&nbsp; world. If this continues, our future&nbsp; generations will be left with nothing. Lakshika Soni , 16, Government High School, Mocha, Madhya Pradesh (Last Wilderness Foundation) Illustration by Sudarshan Shaw

There is no future without forests and rivers, and to save them, we must protect the tiger. Tigers are known as an ‘umbrella species’. Imagine a tiger&nbsp; holding up an umbrella. Under the umbrella is its home, the forest and its&nbsp; plants and animals and the river that lives in the forest — alongside the Adivasis&nbsp; who stay there. In India and across the world, Adivasis and indigenous people&nbsp; have been fierce protectors of the forests they live in, and its wildlife. But these&nbsp; communities are often displaced and forced to move out of forests. This exposes&nbsp; the land to be converted to mines or agricultural land, and puts rivers and&nbsp; wildlife in danger.&nbsp; The community’s rights must be protected so that they can&nbsp; continue to live in their homes, deep in the heart of the forest.

I feel upset about my future on this planet, because we children have to face it the most and I am also feeling sorry for the Earth. Mehran Shafi , 12, Haji Public School, Doda, Jammu and Kashmir Illustration by Kanak Shashi

Global warming is causing the Himalayan region to warm up much faster than the rest of the world. The melting ice forms glacial lakes, which could overflow, causing avalanches and landslides. Flash floods, which arrive with no warning, destroy everything in their path — villages, farms, wildlife and people. While extreme weather events caused by climate change affect everyone, children have more to lose. Children will end up facing about three times as many climate disasters as their grandparents did.

I will try to protect you, Earth, from this changing weather. Bethany , 10, Hope Centre School of Music and Performing Arts, Dimapur, Nagaland Illustration by Ogin Nayam

Every year, Nagaland welcomes the Amur falcons as they make their epic journey of 22,000km, from Africa all the way to Asia and back. Lakhs of them can be seen resting and roosting around Pangti village, where they were once hunted but now are protected and celebrated by the residents. But Nagaland is seeing another migration, one set off by problems arising from climate change. Cultivation cycles get impacted by untimely rainfall. More and more people are leaving their villages, giving rise to a generation of climate refugees.

Dear Earth,&nbsp; Thank you for giving me a home. Poorna Nama , 14, Fateh Public School, Sawai Madhopur, Rajasthan Illustration by Priya Kuriyan

For a barnacle, a whale is home. For a hermit crab, a shell is home. For an orchid, a tree is home. For a tree, a forest is home. For a leopard, a city forest is home. For a butterfly, a garden is home. For a spider, a windowsill is home. For a gecko, a wall is home. Every home matters. For all of us, Earth is home.

I want to save the future of all the children and all the people of future generations. Riddhima Pandey , Climate activist, India I will increase the wealth of the forest. I donʼt want there to be any noise pollution (and water&nbsp; and air pollution) in the future. Mahesh Nayak , 14, VGKK School, BR Hills, Karnataka Illustration by Babakiki

If I could do one thing to stop climate change, I would evaporate the rain and continue sports period. Ayush Jadhav , 11, Akshara High School, Mumbai, Maharashtra Climate change is like a zombie apocalypse. Abhiraj , 16, Akshara High School, Mumbai, Maharashtra Homo sapiens have not yet failed. Yes, we are failing, but there is still time to turn everything around. We can still fix this. Greta Thunberg , Climate justice activist, Sweden We are on Earth to take care of life. We are on Earth to take care of each other. Xiye Bastida , Climate justice activist, Mexico

It’s time for change. Children are standing up, voicing their disappointment, asking for better decisions for a hopeful future. It’s time for action, to make things right, to listen to children. It’s time to listen to you. Together, we can restore our planet, and create a better world for all of us. Illustration by Canato Jimo
