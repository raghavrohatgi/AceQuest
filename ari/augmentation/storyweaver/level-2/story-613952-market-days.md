# Market Days

In the olden days, there were no watches or a calendar for people to follow to keep time.&nbsp; They had their own unique ways of keeping up with the changing days, months and seasons. &nbsp;

The crow of a hen would indicate the start of the day, while the cries of weng weng* signified the end of the day.&nbsp; Changes in the trees meant rain, and the sight of ants marching away with food was also a sign of the rainy season approaching. *Weng weng: cicadas.

There are seven days in a week, but did you know?

For us Jaiñtia people, we have eight days instead of seven!

King Jaiñtiapur assigned market days for every village.

Since then, each village has a market day and every market day has a name.

For example, in Sutnga the market day is known as Khūllau .

Similarly, different villages have different market days. That’s how the eight days of the Pnar calendar based on the market days came to be, and is still in practice till today! About 'Market Days' &nbsp; ‘Market Days,’ is based on the unique Pnar calendar which has eight days instead of the usual seven. &nbsp; In the days when time was observed with the guidance of the ever changing nature in the Jaiñtia Hills, there soon arose a need for a calendar.&nbsp; King Jaiñtia pur assigned separate market days for each village, based on which, the Pnar calendar came into existence.&nbsp; Each day in the calendar denotes the market day of a specific village. A few villages share the same market day. This calendar is followed till date by the people of the&nbsp; Pnar community.
