# Jhōr Khlō

Myrdoh (Chameleon plant) My leaves are green, and my roots are white.&nbsp; Psst! Did you know my roots are edible too?

Tyrkhaŋ (Fiddlehead fern) Come, munch on my leaves, curly and light, they'll fill you with energy so right!

Yaraiñ (Buckwheat) My leaves are wide and my edges&nbsp; are sharp.&nbsp; &nbsp;With salt and pepper,&nbsp; my stems taste superb!

Yali (Nepal Gynura) Hey! Hey!&nbsp; I am long,&nbsp; and my leaves sing&nbsp; a great song&nbsp; about health and &nbsp;wellness all day long.

Jatira (Water Celery) &nbsp;I love water and I grow in damp places. &nbsp;And hey! I have 95% water in me!

lalaŋyar (Spiny Sowthistle) I may be bitter, but did you know I go well with salads? I can also help cleanse your palate!

My edges are pointy, but I promise I'm nice You can have me with noodles, soup or you can make a chutney out of my leaves! Dhania chieh (Thorny Coriander)

Slalachet (Ficus) I'm packed with fibre&nbsp; that's super good for your belly,&nbsp; and&nbsp; guess what?&nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp;I taste really sweet too!

Syrthid (Beggar Ticks) &nbsp;An upset tummy making you feel blue? Fear not!&nbsp; My leaves will make it all go poof!

Disclaimer! Before consuming any of these wild edibles,&nbsp; make sure to consult with an adult first. All the information in this book is taken from the North East Society for Agroecology Support (NESFAS).

Activity &nbsp; &nbsp; Name the three wild edibles that are on this&nbsp; page and note them down. &nbsp; &nbsp; &nbsp;

‘Jhōr Khlō,’ is part of the 45 Meghalaya-contextual storybooks curated under The Forgotten Folklore Project (TFFP), an initiative of the Sauramandala Foundation. &nbsp;The TFFP team is grateful to the North East Society for Agroecology Support (NESFAS) for giving the TFFP team access to information on these local herbs and plants available in the Jaiñtia region of Meghalaya, without which this book would not have been possible.
