# Echoes of Childhood

“Ane, I am going out to play!” Marba called out. “Alright, be back by five and be careful.” “Ke, ane.”

“These look nice and ripe.” “Don’t squash them!” “Coming, coming!” “Oi Bommar, come out fast!”

Splat!&nbsp; Splat! Splat! Splat! “They have started already!” Marba yelled. Splat! Splat! “Wait! Let us get ready,” Bommar cried. “Hahahahah, not a chance!” Yapi giggled.

“Oh yeah! Then take this!” Splat! Splat! Splat! Splat! “Ouch!”&nbsp; “Catch me if you can!” “You can’t get me, haha!”&nbsp; “Yay! We win!” Yapi and Yanga cheer. “Hahahahahaha.”&nbsp; “Of course, it was three against two.&nbsp; We were at&nbsp; a disadvantage,” Marba said. “Anyway, I need a new pipe,”&nbsp; Bommar remarked.

“Let’s go down to the river,” Marba said,&nbsp; “we can make a new pipe for you.” “And also wash up,” Bommar added, “I am all sticky.”

“Wowwww” “Let’s go! Let’s go! The others could be there.”

“Let’s jump in,” Marba said, excited. “We are washing our clothes first,”&nbsp; Yapi&nbsp; called out. “We can’t go back&nbsp; like this.”&nbsp; “Okay, okay,” the boys responded.&nbsp; “Let’s do that.”

“And done!” The boys look proudly at the washed clothes. “Marba, Bommar, are you coming?” Tago called out. “Yes!” They both jumped into the water.

“I am hungry,” Yapi said, drying her hair. “Me too. Let's stop by Abo Tanya’s shop!” said Bommar.&nbsp; “Yes!” everyone cried in unison, “We can get some mithai!”

“Abooooooo!”&nbsp; “Ah, you urchins! Your box of stuff is here with me.”&nbsp; “Ohhhh! We had forgotten about this! Why don’t&nbsp; we play gotti&nbsp; then?” Marba asked.&nbsp; “That’s a great idea! The loser buys us sweets!”&nbsp; Yanga chimed in. “Yay!”

“Ey, that will not be counted!”&nbsp; “I got three!” “Oi Bommar, don’t cheat, okay?”&nbsp; “I am not cheating! It’s not my fault that you suck&nbsp; at this.”&nbsp; “One more left!”&nbsp; “Wohoooo! I win!” Bommar happily cries.

“Hahahaha, here you go, children!” Abo Tanya gives everyone toffees. As they chew on them, they see Takar coming with his gaari.

“Oi Takar, where are you going?” Marba yelled. “I am going to play with this!” Takar said.&nbsp; “All alone, huh? Who’s&nbsp; stopping you?” Takar quips.&nbsp; “Okay!”&nbsp; “Ta-ta, Abo!”&nbsp; “Be careful, kids.”

“My turn, my turn!” “Let us go together! “Hahahaha.”

“Wohooooooooooo... Ahhhhhh” Crash!

“Marba! Bommar! Your Anes are looking for you!”&nbsp; A nyiji passing by said.&nbsp; “Oh no! It’s already getting dark,” Marba exclaimed.&nbsp; “Let’s go!”&nbsp; “We’ll continue this tomorrow!”

“Ah, we forgot about your pipe!” Marba said. “It’s alright, we’ll make one tomorrow!” Bommar replied. “Okay, and tomorrow we will not leave those three!” The two went back home, laughing all the way.

Glossary •&nbsp;Ane: A Galo word for mother •&nbsp;Abo: A Galo word for father or uncle •&nbsp;Gotti: A game of marbles •&nbsp;Nyiji: A Galo word for old man

About the story This story presents a glimpse of the lives of children in a Galo village. The book is based on the childhood games that were played in these villages earlier, which are slowly dying out with the rapid changes taking place in these villages. Drawn from the very personal experiences of the illustrator, these games are not only an homage to childhood and its openness to life but perhaps also present an alternate vision of what childhood could be, in the face of ever-shrinking definitions that we are faced with today.
