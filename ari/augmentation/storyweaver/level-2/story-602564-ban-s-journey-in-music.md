# Ban's Journey in Music

In the rolling East Khasi Hills rests Wahkhen Village, which echoes the songs of Mother Nature.

The village celebrates with music in their hearts. The residents of Wahkhen are skilled at crafting musical instruments.

Children grow up surrounded by music.&nbsp; And young Ban is no different.

Ban is very keen to learn how to play musical instruments and eagerly accompanies his mother to the village's annual festival. “Look, Mei! Pa is playing Ka Bom ,” points Ban at his father.&nbsp; &nbsp;“And there’s Bahbah with ksing shynrang &nbsp;and k ongthei with the ksing kynthei !” says Mei.

Excited to start his own musical journey, Ban attempts to learn some instruments. However… His hands were not strong enough to beat the Bom . “Ouch! This hurts,” says Ban as he tries to play Duitara . And every time he blew the Tangmuri , it would make an awful sound!

“I cannot find the rhythm!” Ban grumbles as he plays the Ksing Kynthei . “Aah, I cannot do this,”&nbsp; a forlorn Ban thinks to himself.

One day, Babu Toki, the founder of the Seiñ Riti school came to visit Ban’s father.&nbsp; “I see your son has not been enrolled at Seiñ Riti ,” says the Elder. “Yes, he has not found an instrument as of yet,” replies Ban’s father.

“Why are you crying, Ban?” asks Babu Toki Ban quickly wipes his tears away, “I am sorry,&nbsp; I didn’t mean to throw the padiah away…”

That's alright. Now tell me, why are you crying?" asks Babu Toki. &nbsp; "I am so angry that I cannot play the instrument! What if...what if I never find my sound?" asks Ban. Babu Toki smiled. “Dear Ban, I too, did not find my sound." &nbsp; “You didn’t?” asks Ban.

Taking a deep breath, Babu Toki explains, "Little Ban, whether or not we can play an instrument, everyone has a sound of their own." "What do you mean?" asks Ban "Music," says Babu Toki, “is played with your heart, not just with your hands.”

A month later, Ban is taking a walk through the woods as he hears a beautiful sound.

He looks up to see a bird high up on the branch of a tree, pecking at its trunk.

"Is this what they call rhythm?" wonders Ban.

With that thought, Ban rushes home.

Once home, Ban takes out his mother’s pots and starts beating on them with sticks.&nbsp; He tries to create his own rhythm.

Ban's family is excited for him. His father holds a pa diah in his hands.&nbsp; "Try this," he says gently.

Dam! Dam! Dam! &nbsp;“I will never stop playing,” exclaims Ban.

Slowly, Ban learns to play each instrument.&nbsp; “Babu Toki was right, music really comes from the heart. Everyone has their own sound.”

"I can’t wait to learn the other instruments too!" said Ban to Babu Toki.&nbsp; Babu Toki grinned and nodded.&nbsp;&nbsp; "And now Ban, you have found your sound.&nbsp; With little time and patience, we can always find our sound!”

Glossary Mei: &nbsp;A Khasi word for mother. Pa: A Khasi word for father. Bahbah:&nbsp; A Khasi word for brother. Ksing Shynrang: &nbsp; A percussion instrument made with wood, leather and parchment. It is played accompanying the all-male dance. While it is designed to be played with sticks, the Ksing Shynrang is often played with hands too. Ksing Kynthei: A percussion instrument made with wood, leather and parchment. It is played accompanying the all-female dance. Unlike the Ksing Shynrang , Ksing Kynthei is played with hands without sticks. Kongthei: &nbsp;A Khasi word for elder sister. Ka Bom: &nbsp;It is a big drum that is made out of wood from a jackfruit tree and covered with the skin of a cow. It is known as the mother instrument of the Khasis. In the olden days, Ka Bom was used for proclamation when the King or the village elders called for a meeting which was then accompanied with folk music and dance. Tangmuri: is a double-reed conical-bore wind instrument used by the Hynniew Trep people of Meghalaya. Padiah: it is a small drum covered with two layers of the skin of a goat. It is played with two thin specially made bamboo sticks. &nbsp; Wahkhen:&nbsp; A village which lies in the Pynursla Tehsil of the East Khasi Hills district in Meghalaya. It lies 42 km away from Shillong, the capital of Meghalaya. Seiñ Riti:&nbsp; is a folk musical academy that was founded by Mr. Komik Khongjirem and runs with the support of the locals from Wahkhen village.
