# Charcoal

“Angu, Ani, my parents are&nbsp;waiting for you both.”

“Don’t loiter away from the house, boys” “Also, let Asu take rest while he is here.” “Bye Ipu, bye&nbsp; Iza”

“Asu is napping again” “Let’s go”

“Hehehe, lets go!” “Ah Watch out, Xuivi!” “I have to find it!” “We do not want to anger Iza later” “He he he”

“Khumsta, I found it! I found it” “Found what?”

“ITU! Yesterday I saw Asu scrubbing his teeth with this”

“Oh Asu does that every morning. He scrubs, scratches and rubs his teeth with it” ‘Scratch? Is Asu’s teeth itchy?’

GRR GRRR SCREECH CRRK CRRKK AHAHAHA HAA!

“Hahahahaa” “Me, let me try!”

Umm? Screek! Huh?

Yuck! Thoo! Yuck WAHHHH!

“Ahh, Hahahaha” “Come, I'll show you how it's done”

“Asu, is that the same thing we&nbsp;have been using?’&nbsp; ‘Fireplace charcoal? Yikes!”

“Yes boys, we choose the softest charcoal to clean our teeth, you'll get sparkling teeth after you're done.”

Brush, brush, brush

“Hahahahaa” “And behold your clothes.” “Hehehehehe”

Glossary Angu: A sumi word for uncle Ani: A sumi word for aunt Ipu: Father/dad in Sumi Iza: Mother/mum in Sumi Asu: Grandfather in Sumi Aizaoo: Surprised/amazed Chiiee: Disgust Itu: ‘This’ in Nagamese

About the Story In the past, fireplace charcoal had been commonly used for oral care hygiene in Nagaland. While it is no longer practiced today, it remains a significant memory. The story of Khumtsa and Xuivi pays homage to this traditional practice, denoting the space it holds today.
