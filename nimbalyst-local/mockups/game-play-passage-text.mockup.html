The Amazon Rainforest
🏷️ Source: National Geographic for Kids

The Amazon Rainforest, often called 'The Lungs of the Earth', is the world's largest tropical rainforest. Covering over 5.5 million square kilometres, it spans across nine countries in South America. Brazil contains about 60% of this vast forest, which produces approximately 20% of the world's oxygen.

The Amazon is home to an extraordinary diversity of life. Scientists estimate that the rainforest houses around 10% of all species on Earth, including 40,000 plant species, 1,300 bird species, and 3,000 types of fish. Many of these species are found nowhere else on the planet, making the Amazon truly unique.

The forest plays a critical role in regulating the global climate. Its trees absorb enormous amounts of carbon dioxide and release oxygen through photosynthesis. The Amazon also creates its own rainfall through a process called transpiration, where trees release water vapour that forms clouds and rain.

Unfortunately, the Amazon Rainforest faces serious threats. Deforestation for agriculture, logging, and urban development has destroyed significant portions of the forest. Scientists warn that if deforestation continues at its current rate, the Amazon could reach a 'tipping point' beyond which it cannot recover.
